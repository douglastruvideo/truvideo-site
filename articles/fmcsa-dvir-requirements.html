FMCSA DVIR Requirements: Everything Fleet Operators Need to Know

Compliance Fleet
TruVideo Team · April 2026 · 8 min read

In This Article

DVIR compliance is one of those areas where the regulations are straightforward on paper but surprisingly easy to get wrong in practice. The requirements themselves are simple: inspect your vehicles, document what you find, fix what's broken, and keep records. The complexity comes from doing this consistently across hundreds of drivers, thousands of vehicles, and millions of miles — while maintaining the documentation trail that proves you did it.

This guide breaks down every FMCSA DVIR requirement, explains how violations impact your CSA scores, and provides a framework for building an inspection program that survives not just routine audits but the intense scrutiny that follows a serious incident. For the foundational concepts, see our DVIR explainer.

The Regulatory Framework

DVIR requirements fall under Title 49 of the Code of Federal Regulations, Part 396 — Inspection, Repair, and Maintenance. The two sections that directly govern DVIRs are 396.11 (the driver's reporting obligation) and 396.13 (the driver's pre-operation review obligation). These sections work together to create a continuous inspection-report-repair cycle for every commercial motor vehicle in your fleet.

It's worth noting that these are federal minimum requirements. States can — and some do — impose additional inspection obligations. California, for example, has a mandatory periodic inspection program (BIT) that goes beyond FMCSA requirements. Always verify your state-level obligations in addition to meeting the federal baseline.

49 CFR 396.11 — Driver Vehicle Inspection Report

This is the core DVIR regulation. Here's what it requires, precisely.

When: At the completion of each day's work, on each vehicle operated during that day. "Day's work" means the end of the driver's duty period, not the end of a calendar day. A driver who works a night shift completes their DVIR at the end of their shift, not at midnight.

What: The report must cover, at minimum, the condition of the following equipment categories. For trucks and truck tractors: parking brake, steering mechanism, lighting devices and reflectors, horn, windshield wipers, rear vision mirrors, coupling devices, wheels and rims, tires, emergency equipment, air compressor, air lines, brake connections, brakes (including trailer brake connections), suspension system, and frame. For buses, the same items plus emergency exits, seats, and related passenger safety equipment.

How: The regulation requires a "report in writing" but explicitly permits electronic formats. The report must identify the vehicle (typically by unit number or license plate), identify the driver, indicate whether defects were found, and specify those defects. If no defects are discovered, the driver must still complete the report indicating the inspection was performed.

What happens next: If the driver reports any defect that would affect safe operation or result in a mechanical breakdown, the motor carrier must repair the defect before permitting the vehicle to operate again. The carrier must certify on the original report (or attached to it) that the repair was performed or that the reported condition doesn't require repair. The motor carrier must retain the original report and the certification of repair for 90 days from the date the report was prepared.

The "no defect" exception

If the driver reports no defects, the carrier is not required to retain the report. However, this creates a documentation gap that smart fleet operators fill anyway. If an accident occurs and the carrier cannot produce a DVIR for the date in question, the absence of documentation can be used against the carrier in litigation. Best practice: retain all DVIRs, including no-defect reports, for at least 90 days. Many carriers retain them for 12 months or longer.

49 CFR 396.13 — Driver Inspection

This regulation addresses what happens before a vehicle is operated — the handoff between the previous driver's DVIR and the next driver's acceptance of the vehicle.

Before driving: No motor carrier shall require or permit a driver to operate a CMV unless the driver is satisfied that the vehicle is in safe operating condition and, if the last DVIR for the vehicle noted any defects, the driver has reviewed the report and signed it to acknowledge review.

The certification chain: If the previous DVIR noted defects, two things must happen before the vehicle operates again. First, the motor carrier must certify that the defects were repaired or don't require repair (this certification can be made by a mechanic, shop foreman, or other qualified person designated by the carrier). Second, the next driver must review both the original DVIR and the repair certification, and sign the report to acknowledge receipt.

This creates a three-signature chain on any DVIR with reported defects: the reporting driver, the repair certifier, and the accepting driver. A break in any link of this chain is a violation.

Who Must Comply

DVIR requirements apply to every motor carrier operating commercial motor vehicles (CMVs) in interstate commerce. A CMV for DVIR purposes includes any vehicle with a gross vehicle weight rating (GVWR) of 10,001 pounds or more, any vehicle designed to transport 9 or more passengers (including the driver) for compensation, or any vehicle designed to transport 16 or more passengers regardless of compensation, or any vehicle transporting hazardous materials requiring placards.

The obligation falls on both the driver (to complete and sign the DVIR) and the motor carrier (to ensure DVIRs are completed, defects are addressed, and records are retained). Both can be cited for violations. In practice, the carrier bears the heavier enforcement burden because they're responsible for the systematic compliance program.

Exemptions

Short-haul drivers operating under the 150-air-mile radius exemption (49 CFR 395.1(e)(2)) are exempt from some hours-of-service requirements but are NOT exempt from DVIR requirements. This is a common misconception that leads to violations. If you operate CMVs, you need DVIRs, regardless of your HOS exemption status.

CSA Scoring and DVIR Violations

DVIR violations feed into the FMCSA's Compliance, Safety, Accountability (CSA) program through the Safety Measurement System (SMS). They fall primarily under the Vehicle Maintenance BASIC, which evaluates carriers on the condition of their equipment and their maintenance practices.

The Vehicle Maintenance BASIC is calculated using a 24-month rolling window of inspection data. Each violation is assigned a severity weight and a time weight (more recent violations count more heavily). Your BASIC score is then converted to a percentile ranking against similar carriers in your peer group (grouped by number of inspections).

FMCSA intervention thresholds: Carriers above the 75th percentile in the Vehicle Maintenance BASIC receive warning letters. Above the 80th percentile triggers targeted investigations. Persistently high scores can result in cooperative safety plans, compliance reviews, and ultimately, proposed operating authority revocation.

Common DVIR-related violations and their severity weights:

  • Failing to require drivers to prepare DVIRs (396.11(a)) — this is a systematic violation with high severity weight because it indicates a programmatic failure, not a single missed report
  • Operating a vehicle with a known defect not certified as repaired (396.13(a)) — high severity because it means the carrier knowingly operated an unsafe vehicle
  • Failing to correct defects noted on DVIR (396.11(c)) — high severity for the same reason
  • Incomplete DVIR or missing required information (396.11(a)) — moderate severity
  • Failing to retain DVIRs for the required 90 days (396.11(c)) — moderate severity

Preparing for a Compliance Review

A compliance review (CR) is an in-depth examination of your operation by FMCSA investigators. During a CR, the investigator will request DVIR records as a standard part of their examination. Here's what they're looking for and how to prepare.

Record completeness. The investigator will sample DVIRs from the review period and verify that each contains all required information: vehicle identification, driver identification, date, inspection results, and — if defects were noted — repair certification and accepting driver acknowledgment. Electronic records must be printable on demand.

Defect resolution timeliness. For DVIRs that note defects, the investigator will verify that repairs were certified before the vehicle operated again. This is where the timestamp matters — if the DVIR was completed at 6:00 PM and the vehicle departed at 6:00 AM the next morning, the repair certification must fall between those times.

Pattern analysis. Investigators look for systemic issues. Are there periods where DVIRs are missing entirely? Are certain vehicles consistently operating without reports? Are defects being reported but not repaired? A pattern of non-compliance is treated much more seriously than an isolated missed report.

Best practice for audit readiness: Run your own internal audit quarterly. Pull a random sample of 25-50 DVIRs and verify completeness, repair certification chain, and retention. Fix any gaps you find before an investigator finds them. Carriers that can demonstrate a proactive audit program receive more favorable treatment during compliance reviews.

Electronic DVIRs and Regulatory Acceptance

The FMCSA explicitly permits electronic DVIRs. The relevant guidance states that electronic reports are acceptable provided they can be produced in written form upon request and meet the same content requirements as paper reports. There is no special certification or approval process for electronic DVIR systems — if the system captures and retains the required information, it's compliant.

Electronic DVIR systems offer several compliance advantages over paper. They enforce mandatory fields, preventing incomplete reports. They timestamp and geolocate entries, proving when and where the inspection occurred. They automate the routing of defect reports to maintenance and track repair certification in a way that prevents the vehicle from being dispatched before sign-off. And they provide instant access to records during audits, eliminating the filing cabinet search.

For fleet operators evaluating electronic DVIR platforms, the compliance-critical features are: mandatory field enforcement, tamper-evident timestamps, automated repair notification and tracking, digital signature capture for all three links in the certification chain, and export capabilities that can produce records in the format investigators expect.

Building a Compliant Inspection Program

A compliant DVIR program requires three components: clear policy, consistent execution, and systematic oversight.

Policy. Document your DVIR requirements in your safety policy manual. Specify who is responsible for what, when DVIRs must be completed, how defects are reported and tracked, and what the consequences are for non-compliance. This policy should be reviewed annually and updated as regulations change. Every driver should acknowledge the policy in writing during onboarding and during annual safety refreshers.

Execution. Provide drivers with the tools and training to complete DVIRs correctly and efficiently. Whether you use paper forms or an electronic platform, the training should cover: what to inspect, how to document findings, how to rate severity, and the importance of completing the report even when no defects are found. Conduct ride-alongs periodically to verify that inspections are being performed thoroughly, not just documented reflexively.

Oversight. Assign someone — a safety director, compliance officer, or fleet manager — the specific responsibility of monitoring DVIR compliance. This person should track completion rates by driver and vehicle, verify defect resolution timeliness, conduct periodic record audits, and escalate persistent non-compliance to management. Without dedicated oversight, even well-intentioned programs drift toward non-compliance over time.

The most advanced fleet operations are adding a fourth component: visual evidence. Video-documented inspections don't just satisfy the regulatory minimum — they create an evidentiary standard that exceeds what paper or checkbox-based systems can provide. When a driver records a video walkaround showing every inspected component, the carrier has proof that the inspection was thorough, not just that a form was completed. In post-accident litigation, this distinction can be worth millions.

FMCSA compliance isn't about surviving audits. It's about building an inspection culture where safety is the default, documentation is automatic, and defects get fixed before they become incidents. The regulations are the floor. Best practice is the ceiling.
