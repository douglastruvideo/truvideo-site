Pre-Trip Inspection Checklist: The Complete FMCSA-Compliant Guide

Guide Compliance Fleet
TruVideo Team · April 2026 · 8 min read

In This Article

Every commercial vehicle trip in the United States begins — or should begin — with a pre-trip inspection. It's a federal requirement under FMCSA regulations, a critical safety practice, and one of the most common areas where carriers accumulate CSA violations during roadside inspections. A thorough pre-trip takes 15-20 minutes. Skipping it can cost you thousands in fines, days of out-of-service downtime, and in the worst case, lives.

This guide provides a complete, FMCSA-compliant pre-trip inspection checklist organized by vehicle area, with time estimates for each section and specific attention to the items that most frequently trigger roadside violations. Whether you're a new CDL holder learning the process or a fleet safety manager building a training program, this is the reference you need. For background on the regulatory framework, see our complete DVIR guide.

Why the Pre-Trip Inspection Matters

The pre-trip inspection is your last opportunity to catch a mechanical issue before it becomes a roadside emergency. FMCSA data consistently shows that the majority of out-of-service violations discovered during roadside inspections are for conditions that a proper pre-trip would have identified: brake adjustment issues, tire problems, lighting failures, and fluid leaks.

Beyond compliance, there's a straightforward safety argument. A tire blowout at highway speed, a brake failure on a mountain grade, or a steering component failure in traffic can be catastrophic. The pre-trip isn't bureaucracy — it's the process that prevents those scenarios. Experienced drivers don't resent the pre-trip. They rely on it.

Engine Compartment Check

Estimated time: 3-4 minutes

Start with the engine compartment before anything else. The engine should be off and cool enough to safely check fluid levels and inspect components.

  • Engine oil level — Check dipstick; look for dark discoloration that suggests overdue service. Check the ground beneath the engine for active leaks.
  • Coolant level — Verify the reservoir is between min and max marks. Never open a pressurized radiator cap on a hot engine. Look for cracked or swollen hoses and white residue at connection points that indicates slow leaks.
  • Power steering fluid — Check the reservoir level. Low fluid combined with steering difficulty is an immediate out-of-service condition.
  • Belts and hoses — Check for fraying, cracking, glazing, or excessive slack on belts. Hoses should be firm but pliable, with no bulges, soft spots, or abrasion marks where they contact other components.
  • Air compressor — Visually inspect for oil leaks around the compressor. Listen for unusual noises during the air system build-up test (performed later from the cab).
  • Wiring — Look for exposed, frayed, or melted wiring. Electrical fires are among the most dangerous equipment failures on commercial vehicles.
  • Leaks — Check for any fluid pooling or active dripping: oil, coolant, fuel, power steering fluid, or brake fluid. Any active leak warrants investigation before departure.

Cab and Interior Check

Estimated time: 3-4 minutes

Enter the cab and work systematically through interior controls and instruments.

  • Seat belt — Inspect webbing for cuts or fraying. Verify the buckle latches and releases properly. A non-functional seat belt is an out-of-service condition.
  • Mirrors — Adjust all mirrors (flat, convex, hood-mounted) for proper visibility. Check for cracks or loose mounting that affects stability.
  • Windshield and wipers — Check for cracks in the driver's line of sight (any crack larger than 3/4 inch in the sweep area is a violation). Test wipers for proper operation and blade condition. Verify washer fluid level and spray function.
  • Horn — Test for proper function. A non-working horn is a common and easily avoidable violation.
  • Instrument panel — With the key on (engine off), verify all warning lights illuminate during the bulb check. Note any lights that stay on after engine start — particularly oil pressure, coolant temperature, air pressure, and check engine lights.
  • Air brake system — Start the engine and build air pressure to governor cut-out (typically 120-140 PSI). With the engine off and brakes released, check that air pressure doesn't drop more than 3 PSI in one minute (single vehicle) or 4 PSI in one minute (combination). Pump the brake pedal to reduce pressure and verify the low air warning activates between 55-75 PSI. Continue pumping to verify the spring brakes engage before pressure drops below 20 PSI.
  • Parking brake — With the parking brake set, gently attempt to move the vehicle in a low gear. The vehicle should not move. Release and verify the vehicle moves freely.
  • Steering play — With the engine running, check for excessive play in the steering wheel. For power steering vehicles, FMCSA allows no more than 2 inches of free play on a 20-inch steering wheel.
  • Emergency equipment — Verify the presence and condition of fire extinguisher (check charge gauge and inspection date), three reflective triangles, and spare fuses (if applicable to the vehicle).

Exterior Walkaround

Estimated time: 5-7 minutes

The walkaround is the most comprehensive portion of the inspection. Work in a consistent pattern — most drivers go front-to-back on the driver's side, across the rear, and back-to-front on the passenger's side — so you develop muscle memory and never skip an area.

  • Headlights and signals — Check low beams, high beams, turn signals, and hazard flashers. Have someone confirm brake lights and turn signals at the rear, or use a reflective surface. Non-functioning lights are the single most common violation category during roadside inspections.
  • Tires — Check all tires for proper inflation (use a gauge, not a kick test), tread depth (minimum 4/32" on steer tires, 2/32" on all others), sidewall damage, exposed cords, uneven wear patterns, and objects lodged in the tread. Check valve stems for damage or missing caps. Verify lug nuts are present, not loose, and show no rust streaks radiating outward (a sign of loosening).
  • Wheels and rims — Look for cracks, dents, or weld repairs on rims. Check for missing or broken studs. Inspect hub seals for leaks — oil running down the inside of a wheel indicates a failing hub seal.
  • Suspension — Inspect spring hangers, leaf springs (look for shifted, cracked, or missing leaves), spring mounts, and shock absorbers (look for leaks). Air ride vehicles: check air bags for damage, leaks, or improper inflation. The vehicle should sit level when loaded.
  • Brakes — Visually inspect brake drums/rotors for cracks. Check brake hoses and lines for chafing, leaks, or damage. On S-cam brakes, check pushrod travel (with brakes applied) against the vehicle's adjustment limit — out-of-adjustment brakes are the most common out-of-service violation in FMCSA roadside inspections. Look for brake lining material contaminated with oil or grease.
  • Frame and body — Check the frame for cracks, especially around spring mounts and fifth-wheel mounting points. Look for loose or missing bolts. Inspect mud flaps for proper positioning and securement.
  • Fuel system — Check fuel tanks for leaks, especially at the filler neck and around mounting straps. Verify the fuel cap seals properly. Inspect fuel lines for chafing where they run along the frame.
  • Exhaust system — Check for leaks, especially where exhaust components pass near the cab or fuel system. Loose, broken, or missing exhaust components are both a violation and a carbon monoxide hazard.

Coupling System (Tractor-Trailer)

Estimated time: 2-3 minutes

  • Fifth wheel — Check that the fifth wheel plate is properly greased. Verify the locking jaws are closed and secured around the kingpin. Check for visible space between the upper and lower fifth wheel — any gap indicates an improper coupling. Tug test: with the trailer brakes set and tractor brakes released, gently pull against the coupling to verify it holds.
  • Air and electrical lines — Check glad hands for proper connection and no air leaks. Inspect air lines for chafing, kinks, or cuts along their entire length. Verify the electrical cord is securely connected and not dragging. Check that lines have enough slack for turns but aren't so long they'll catch on obstacles.
  • Kingpin — Visually inspect for cracks or excessive wear. A damaged kingpin is an immediate out-of-service condition that can cause a trailer separation.
  • Sliding fifth wheel — If equipped, verify the slide is properly locked in position with all locking pins engaged. Check the slide mechanism for damage or obstruction.

Trailer Inspection

Estimated time: 3-4 minutes

  • Landing gear — Fully raised and secured with the crank handle stowed. Low-hanging landing gear is a common cause of damage and can snag on road surfaces or railroad crossings.
  • Doors — Verify all doors (rear swing, roll-up, side) open, close, and secure properly. Check door seals for refrigerated trailers. Confirm required placards are in place if hauling hazmat.
  • Lights and reflectors — Check all trailer-mounted lights: marker lights, clearance lights, side reflectors, tail lights, brake lights, turn signals, and license plate light. Reflective tape or conspicuity markings should be intact along the sides and rear.
  • Tires, wheels, brakes, and suspension — Repeat the same checks performed on the tractor for every trailer axle. Trailer brakes are subject to the same out-of-service criteria as tractor brakes.
  • Load securement — If loaded, verify cargo is properly secured per FMCSA requirements. Check tie-downs, chains, straps, and blocking/bracing. Confirm the load doesn't exceed axle weight ratings or shift risk.

Post-Trip Inspection Differences

The post-trip inspection covers the same items as the pre-trip but adds a critical dimension: conditions that developed during the day's operation. During your post-trip, pay particular attention to anything that felt, sounded, or handled differently during the drive. Vibrations that weren't there in the morning, new air system noises, changes in braking feel, unusual smells — these are early warnings of problems that may not yet be visible on a walkaround but need to be documented in your DVIR.

The post-trip DVIR is also where your documentation creates legal protection. If you report a defect at the end of your shift and the carrier fails to address it before the next driver operates the vehicle, the liability shifts from you to the carrier. Thorough post-trip reporting isn't just safety — it's self-protection.

Top 10 Roadside Violations to Avoid

Based on FMCSA roadside inspection data, these are the violations that most frequently result in citations or out-of-service orders. Each is detectable during a proper pre-trip.

  1. Brake adjustment — The most common OOS violation. Check pushrod travel on every axle.
  2. Inoperable lighting — Tail lights, brake lights, and turn signals fail frequently. Check every light, every trip.
  3. Tire condition — Tread depth, inflation, sidewall damage, and exposed cords. Steer tire violations are taken most seriously.
  4. Brake hose/tubing chafing — Lines that rub against frame components will eventually fail. Look for wear spots on every line.
  5. Oil/grease leaks — Hub seal failures and engine leaks that contact hot exhaust components are fire hazards.
  6. Air line leaks — Listen for audible leaks with the engine off and system pressurized. Check every glad hand connection.
  7. Windshield condition — Cracks in the sweep area are violations. Chips left unrepaired become cracks quickly.
  8. Exhaust system leaks — Particularly where exhaust passes near the cab. Carbon monoxide intrusion is a serious safety issue.
  9. Missing/defective reflectors — Easy to overlook, easy to fix, and commonly cited during nighttime inspections.
  10. Steering system defects — Excessive play, damaged components, and power steering leaks. Any steering defect is treated as high-severity.

Video Documentation: Beyond the Checkbox

The traditional pre-trip checklist — whether paper or electronic — has an inherent limitation: it records what the driver says they checked, not what they actually observed. A checkbox marked "tires: OK" provides no evidence of what the tire condition actually was at the time of inspection.

Video documentation changes this fundamentally. When a driver records their walkaround, the video captures exactly what was visible at the time of inspection — tire tread depth, brake component condition, fluid levels, lighting function, coupling integrity. This creates an auditable, timestamped record that serves multiple purposes: compliance evidence for regulators, liability protection in post-accident litigation, and a data source for AI-powered condition analysis that can track component wear across inspections.

For fleet operators managing hundreds or thousands of vehicles, video-documented inspections also provide a quality assurance layer. Safety managers can review inspection videos to verify thoroughness, identify drivers who need additional training, and catch developing maintenance issues before they become roadside failures.

A pre-trip inspection is only as valuable as the evidence it creates. Checklists prove you completed a form. Video proves you inspected a vehicle.

The 15-20 minutes you invest in a thorough pre-trip inspection is the most consequential time in your entire driving day. Make it count, document it properly, and treat it as the safety-critical process it is — not a form to be filled out before you turn the key.
