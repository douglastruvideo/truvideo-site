Field Service Technician: The Complete Career & Operations Guide (2026)

The field service technician is one of the most essential — and consistently underestimated — roles in the modern economy. These are the professionals who show up when a hospital's imaging equipment fails, when a cell tower loses signal, when an industrial chiller shuts down mid-production, or when a homeowner's HVAC system stops working in August. They diagnose complex problems under pressure, often alone, often without a stable internet connection, and often with a customer watching every move.

As of 2026, the global field service management market is valued at over $5 billion and growing. Demand for qualified field service technicians is outpacing supply across nearly every vertical — HVAC, telecommunications, medical devices, utilities, and industrial equipment. Yet despite this demand, there are surprisingly few comprehensive resources that explain what the role actually involves, what it pays, what skills it requires, and where it is headed.

This guide is built to fill that gap. Whether you are exploring a career as a field service technician, managing a team of them, or trying to understand how modern tools are reshaping the role, this is the complete reference: what FSTs do, what they earn, what certifications matter, how a typical day unfolds, and how AI and video technology are transforming the job in 2026.

What Is a Field Service Technician?

A field service technician (FST) is a skilled professional who travels to customer locations — homes, businesses, data centers, hospitals, manufacturing plants, or remote infrastructure sites — to install, inspect, maintain, repair, and troubleshoot equipment and systems. Unlike bench technicians who work in controlled shop environments, FSTs operate in the field, which means adapting to unpredictable conditions, working with limited resources, and representing their company directly to the customer.

The title itself is an umbrella. Field service technicians work across a broad range of industries, and the specific systems they work on vary dramatically:

  • HVAC technicians install and service heating, ventilation, and air conditioning systems in residential and commercial buildings. They handle refrigerant management, ductwork, thermostats, compressors, and air quality systems.
  • Telecommunications technicians install and maintain broadband infrastructure, fiber optic networks, wireless towers, and customer-premise equipment for internet service providers and carriers.
  • Medical equipment technicians (also called biomedical technicians or BMETs) service imaging equipment, patient monitoring systems, surgical tools, and lab instruments in hospitals and clinics. This role carries significant regulatory weight given FDA oversight.
  • Industrial equipment technicians keep manufacturing and production machinery running — CNC machines, conveyor systems, robotics, presses, and process control equipment.
  • Utility technicians work on power distribution infrastructure, smart meters, substations, and water systems for electric, gas, and water utilities.
  • Elevator and escalator technicians install, maintain, and repair vertical transport systems in commercial and residential buildings.
  • IT and network technicians deploy and maintain on-premise network hardware, servers, and security systems at enterprise customer locations.

What unites all of these roles is a common operational model: a work order is dispatched, a technician is routed to a location, the technician diagnoses and resolves an issue (or completes a scheduled service), and the visit is documented. The specifics change industry by industry, but the framework is consistent.

Field service technicians are the face of the companies they represent. Every site visit is a customer experience, a safety event, and an operational record — all at once.

Core Responsibilities

The day-to-day responsibilities of a field service technician span technical work, customer interaction, documentation, and compliance. A strong technician is not just technically capable — they are organized, communicative, safety-conscious, and reliable.

Installation and Commissioning

Many FSTs spend a significant portion of their time on new installations. This includes physically mounting or positioning equipment, running wiring or piping, configuring software and firmware, calibrating sensors and controls, and verifying that the system performs to specification before handing it off to the customer. A clean, well-documented installation sets the foundation for every future service call on that asset.

Preventive Maintenance

Scheduled preventive maintenance (PM) visits are the backbone of recurring revenue for field service organizations. During a PM, technicians follow a structured checklist: inspecting wear parts, cleaning filters or components, lubricating moving parts, testing safety controls, updating firmware, and logging system performance metrics. The goal is to catch problems before they become failures — extending asset life and reducing customer downtime.

Corrective Repair and Troubleshooting

When a system fails, field service technicians are dispatched on a corrective or emergency work order. This is where diagnostic skill matters most. The technician must gather information from the customer, observe the symptom, isolate the root cause, determine the repair path, source or retrieve the correct parts, execute the repair, and verify the fix before leaving the site. In industries like medical equipment or utilities, speed and precision are not optional — they are life-and-safety requirements.

Customer Communication and Education

FSTs are often the only human face their company's customers see regularly. That means they carry a customer service burden alongside their technical role. Effective field service technicians explain what they found, what they did, and what the customer should watch for — in language the customer can understand, not technical jargon. They answer questions, set expectations about repair timelines, and often identify upsell opportunities for service contracts or equipment upgrades.

Documentation and Reporting

Every site visit must be documented. Documentation typically includes: time of arrival and departure, work performed (parts replaced, adjustments made, readings taken), equipment status before and after service, parts used (for inventory and billing), customer signature or acknowledgment, and any follow-up actions required. Accurate documentation protects the technician, protects the company, and feeds the data systems that drive scheduling, inventory, and planning.

Parts and Inventory Management

Field service technicians are often responsible for managing the inventory in their service van or truck. This means knowing what parts they carry, replenishing stock after use, ordering parts in advance for scheduled jobs, and avoiding carrying excess inventory that ties up capital and adds weight. Poor parts management is one of the leading causes of repeat visits — showing up to a job without the right part wastes time, frustrates customers, and hurts first-time fix rates.

Safety Compliance

Depending on the industry, field service work involves working with high-voltage electricity, pressurized systems, hazardous refrigerants, confined spaces, or heights. Safety compliance is not a box to check — it is a daily practice. Technicians are responsible for following lockout/tagout (LOTO) procedures, wearing appropriate personal protective equipment (PPE), conducting job hazard analyses before starting work, and reporting incidents or near-misses through proper channels.

Required Skills & Certifications

Field service technicians need a blend of hard technical skills, job-specific certifications, and the soft skills that make the difference between a technician who gets the job done and one who builds lasting customer relationships.

Hard Technical Skills

The specific technical knowledge required depends on the industry, but across the board, strong FSTs share a common foundation:

  • Electrical fundamentals: Reading schematics and wiring diagrams, using multimeters and clamp meters, understanding AC/DC circuits, diagnosing motor and control system faults.
  • Mechanical aptitude: Comfort with hand tools, power tools, torque specs, and mechanical systems including belts, bearings, pumps, and compressors.
  • Diagnostic thinking: The ability to work through failure systematically — forming a hypothesis, testing it, ruling out causes, and zeroing in on the root issue rather than swapping parts randomly.
  • Systems knowledge: Deep familiarity with the specific equipment and systems in their vertical. An HVAC technician must understand refrigeration cycles, psychrometrics, and control sequences. A biomedical technician must understand imaging physics or patient monitoring protocols.
  • Digital tools proficiency: Modern FSTs use laptops, tablets, or smartphones to access work orders, service manuals, parts catalogs, and remote diagnostics tools. Comfort with technology is increasingly non-negotiable.
  • Basic networking: Even technicians who are not IT specialists increasingly encounter networked equipment — smart thermostats, IoT-connected machines, remotely monitored medical devices. A baseline understanding of IP addressing and connectivity is valuable in nearly every field service vertical.

Key Certifications

Certifications signal competence, meet regulatory requirements, and in some cases are legally required to perform certain work:

  • EPA Section 608 Certification: Required by federal law for any technician who purchases or handles regulated refrigerants (R-22, R-410A, R-32, and others). HVAC technicians at minimum need a Type I or Type II certification, and most pursue the Universal certification. The exam covers refrigerant handling, recovery, and environmental regulations under the Clean Air Act.
  • OSHA 10 and OSHA 30: The OSHA 10-Hour and 30-Hour outreach training programs are not federally mandated for all field service workers, but many employers require them — particularly in construction-adjacent and industrial settings. They demonstrate baseline safety awareness and hazard recognition.
  • CompTIA A+ and Network+: For FSTs working in IT services, managed services, or technology infrastructure, CompTIA certifications are the industry standard for demonstrating hardware and networking competence. They are vendor-neutral and widely recognized.
  • NATE Certification (North American Technician Excellence): The leading independent certification for HVAC technicians in the US. NATE-certified technicians demonstrate verified competency through proctored exams. Many utility rebate programs and manufacturers require NATE certification for warranty work.
  • BICSI Certifications: For telecommunications and structured cabling technicians, BICSI's Installer and Technician credentials are the recognized standard for low-voltage cabling infrastructure work.
  • CBET (Certified Biomedical Equipment Technician): Administered by the Association for the Advancement of Medical Instrumentation (AAMI), the CBET credential is the gold standard for healthcare equipment technicians. It covers electronics, physiology, and the specific regulatory environment of medical devices.
  • NFPA 70E Electrical Safety Training: For technicians who work on or near energized electrical equipment, NFPA 70E training on arc flash hazards and safe work practices is increasingly standard — and in some industrial settings, required.
  • Manufacturer Certifications: Many equipment manufacturers — Carrier, Trane, Siemens, GE HealthCare, and others — offer their own technical training and certification programs. These are highly valued because they are often required to perform warranty repairs and grant access to proprietary technical resources and parts.

Soft Skills That Define Top Technicians

Technical skills get you hired. Soft skills determine how far you advance and how much customers request you by name:

  • Communication: The ability to explain technical findings to non-technical customers clearly and without condescension. This includes written communication for service reports as well as verbal communication at the job site.
  • Problem-solving under pressure: Field service rarely goes exactly as planned. Equipment is in worse shape than expected, the right part is unavailable, or a simple job reveals a more complex underlying issue. Top technicians stay calm, think clearly, and adapt.
  • Time management: FSTs are often responsible for managing their own daily schedule, deciding how long to spend on a job before escalating, and balancing multiple work orders. Poor time management has a cascading effect on the entire dispatch queue.
  • Attention to detail: Whether recording meter readings, documenting parts used, or torquing a fitting to spec, the details matter. Mistakes in the field can create safety hazards, warranty issues, or billing errors.
  • Customer empathy: A broken piece of equipment is often a disruption to someone's business or comfort. Technicians who acknowledge the inconvenience, communicate clearly about the timeline, and demonstrate that they care build the loyalty that drives service contract renewals.
  • Self-direction: Unlike office roles, field service involves long stretches of solo work. The best technicians are motivated, organized, and proactive without needing constant supervision.

The Daily Workflow

No two days in field service are identical — but most follow a recognizable rhythm. Here is what a day in the life of a field service technician typically looks like in 2026.

Start of Day: Preparation and Dispatch Review

Before the first site visit, a prepared technician reviews their work order queue for the day. Modern field service management (FSM) software pushes the day's assignments to a mobile app, complete with customer contact information, equipment history, service notes from previous visits, and directions. The technician checks their van inventory against the parts needed, flags any shortages with dispatch, and routes their day for maximum efficiency.

In high-performing field service organizations, this pre-day review is where AI-assisted scheduling tools provide estimated job durations, flag high-priority or high-risk accounts, and surface relevant technical notes — reducing the time technicians spend figuring out what they need to know before they arrive.

First Call: Arrival and Assessment

At the first job site, the technician introduces themselves, confirms the reported issue with the customer or site contact, and reviews the equipment's service history before touching anything. This initial assessment phase is critical — the information gathered in the first five minutes often determines how efficient the repair will be. A thorough technician asks the right questions: When did the problem start? Was there anything unusual before the failure? Has this happened before?

In an era of video documentation, the arrival walkthrough is also when many technicians capture pre-service video of the equipment's condition — establishing a timestamped visual record before any work begins. This protects the technician from claims of pre-existing damage and gives the customer a clear baseline.

Diagnosis and Repair

With the symptom confirmed, the technician moves into diagnosis. This is the most technically demanding part of the job and where experience pays the greatest dividends. Skilled technicians develop pattern recognition over years of working on the same equipment families — they know which components fail first, which symptoms point to which root causes, and which diagnostic shortcuts are reliable versus misleading.

Once the root cause is identified, the technician determines the repair path: what parts are needed, whether they are on the truck, what the labor steps are, and approximately how long it will take. If parts need to be ordered, the technician communicates a timeline and may need to implement a temporary workaround to restore at least partial function.

Documentation During and After the Visit

Documentation is not a post-job afterthought — it is woven throughout the visit. Modern technicians enter meter readings, parts usage, and notes in real time via mobile app rather than trying to reconstruct everything from memory at the end of the day. The best field service organizations have moved away from paper-based documentation entirely, replacing it with mobile-first workflows that capture structured data at each step of the job.

Before leaving, the technician reviews the completed work order with the customer, obtains a signature or digital acknowledgment, and confirms whether any follow-up actions are needed. Technicians who communicate clearly at job completion dramatically reduce the volume of callbacks and customer complaints.

Mid-Day: Managing Multiple Calls

Most field service technicians complete between three and six jobs per day depending on job complexity and geography. Between calls, they are in contact with dispatch for any schedule changes or emergency additions, coordinating parts delivery if needed, and managing their route in real time. Modern dispatch platforms route technicians dynamically based on traffic, job completion times, and priority changes — a significant evolution from the static paper schedules of even a decade ago.

End of Day: Wrap-Up and Reporting

At the end of the day, technicians complete any remaining documentation, return to the service depot or warehouse to restock parts, and review the next day's queue. In many organizations, technicians also complete a vehicle inspection log for their service vehicle. End-of-day reports feed scheduling, billing, and inventory systems — making the quality of documentation a business-critical function, not just an administrative requirement.

Essential Tools & Technology

The toolkit of a field service technician in 2026 spans physical instruments, digital platforms, and an increasingly powerful set of AI-assisted capabilities.

Physical Tools

The specific instruments depend on the trade, but a well-equipped FST typically carries:

  • Digital multimeters and clamp meters for electrical diagnostics
  • Manifold gauge sets or digital gauge manifolds for refrigerant pressure readings
  • Thermal imaging cameras for non-contact temperature inspection
  • Vibration analyzers for rotating equipment health assessments
  • Insulation resistance testers (megohmmeters) for motor and cable integrity testing
  • Laptop or tablet with manufacturer diagnostic software
  • Oscilloscopes for waveform analysis on electronic control systems
  • Standard hand tools, power tools, and trade-specific specialty tools

Field Service Management (FSM) Software

FSM platforms are the operational backbone of field service organizations. Solutions like ServiceMax, Salesforce Field Service, IFS, ServiceNow Field Service Management, and FieldEdge handle scheduling, dispatch, work order management, customer records, and reporting. From the technician's perspective, the FSM mobile app is their primary interface — it is where they receive assignments, access service history, record work, and communicate with dispatch.

IoT and Remote Monitoring

An increasing share of the equipment FSTs service is connected to remote monitoring systems. IoT sensors on HVAC units, industrial machines, and utility equipment feed data to cloud platforms that can alert service organizations to anomalies before they become failures. This is shifting field service toward a predictive model: instead of responding to breakdowns, technicians are dispatched when sensor data indicates a component is trending toward failure. The result is fewer emergency calls, fewer customer disruptions, and better first-time fix rates.

Augmented Reality (AR) Assistance

AR is moving from pilot program to practical tool in field service. Smart glasses and mobile AR applications allow technicians to overlay wiring diagrams, part numbers, and step-by-step repair procedures onto their physical view of the equipment. Remote expert platforms — where a senior technician or engineer can see through the field technician's camera and annotate what they see in real time — are reducing the need for escalations and enabling less-experienced technicians to handle more complex jobs.

Video Documentation and AI Inspection Tools

Video has become the most important documentation medium in field service. A photo tells you what something looked like at one moment. A video tells you the complete story: the condition of the equipment on arrival, the work being performed, the before-and-after state, and the system test at job completion. Video documentation is verifiable, timestamped, and far more defensible than written notes in any dispute.

Platforms like TruVideo have built AI-assisted inspection workflows specifically for field service environments. Rather than relying on technicians to remember to document the right things, structured video workflows guide technicians through a standardized capture process — ensuring every critical component is recorded, every reading is visible, and every job produces a consistent, auditable record.

Salary & Career Path

Field service technicians are well compensated relative to the education and training required, and the career path offers meaningful upward mobility for those who develop their skills and take on greater responsibility.

Field Service Technician Salary Ranges (2026)

Compensation varies significantly by industry, geography, specialization, and experience level. Based on Bureau of Labor Statistics data and industry surveys, here is a representative overview:

  • Entry-level FST (0–2 years): $38,000–$52,000 per year. This range typically applies to technicians who have completed a trade program or apprenticeship but are still developing their independent diagnostic skills. Many companies supplement base pay with overtime, which can add $5,000–$15,000 annually in busy seasons.
  • Mid-level FST (3–7 years): $52,000–$72,000 per year. With a few years of experience and one or more trade certifications, technicians can command significantly higher base salaries and are often eligible for performance bonuses tied to first-time fix rates, customer satisfaction scores, and revenue generated.
  • Senior FST (8+ years): $72,000–$95,000 per year. Senior technicians who specialize in complex or high-value equipment categories — medical imaging, industrial controls, mission-critical HVAC for data centers — can reach the top of this range and beyond. Specialized knowledge is a significant salary multiplier.
  • Total compensation with benefits: Most full-time field service positions include a service vehicle or vehicle allowance, fuel card, company-provided tools and equipment, smartphone or tablet, uniforms, health insurance, and retirement benefits. When these are factored in, total compensation packages often run $10,000–$25,000 higher than base salary alone suggests.

By industry, HVAC and refrigeration technicians, biomedical equipment technicians, and specialized industrial equipment technicians tend to earn at the higher end of the range. Residential appliance and general consumer electronics technicians tend to fall at the lower end.

Geographic Variation

Field service technician salaries track closely with cost of living and local labor market demand. Major metropolitan areas in California, New York, Texas, and the Pacific Northwest tend to offer the highest absolute salaries. However, rural and remote assignments often come with premium pay — technicians willing to service oil and gas infrastructure, wind farms, or remote utility sites can earn significantly above market rates.

Career Progression

Field service is a genuine career, not just a job category. The typical progression looks like this:

  1. Apprentice / Helper: Assisting experienced technicians on the job while completing formal trade training. This stage develops foundational skills and professional habits.
  2. Entry-Level Technician: Handling routine maintenance and simple repairs independently, with escalation support available from senior colleagues.
  3. Journey-Level Technician: Independently handling the full range of work orders in their specialty, including complex diagnostics and emergency calls.
  4. Senior / Lead Technician: Handling the most complex jobs, mentoring junior technicians, and sometimes taking on informal team lead responsibilities for a geographic territory.
  5. Field Service Engineer: In many organizations, top technical performers transition to a hybrid role that combines field work with technical support, training, and systems design. This is common in medical equipment and industrial automation.
  6. Service Manager or Operations Manager: Managing a team of technicians — handling scheduling, performance management, customer escalations, and business development for a service territory. This is the most common management path for high-performing FSTs.
  7. Technical Trainer or Application Specialist: Leveraging deep technical knowledge to train other technicians or work directly with customers on complex applications. This path suits technicians who enjoy teaching and are strong communicators.
  8. Regional or National Service Director: Overseeing multiple service territories, managing multiple service managers, and contributing to strategic decisions about staffing, tooling, and service offerings.

For technicians who want to stay technical rather than move into management, many organizations now offer individual contributor advancement tracks — distinguishing between a "Field Service Engineer Level III" and a "Service Manager" as parallel career paths rather than a single ladder.

How AI & Video Are Changing the Role

The field service technician role is in the middle of its most significant transformation in decades. The shift is not about replacing technicians with robots — it is about giving technicians tools that make them faster, smarter, and better protected.

AI-Assisted Diagnostics

Machine learning models trained on equipment sensor data, service history, and failure patterns are beginning to surface real-time guidance during service calls. When a technician connects a diagnostic tool to a piece of equipment, AI-assisted platforms can compare the readings against millions of similar data points and suggest the most likely failure modes and repair paths. This is especially valuable for technicians who are newer to a specific equipment family — it puts collective institutional knowledge at their fingertips rather than requiring them to memorize it.

Some field service organizations have deployed AI chat assistants that technicians can query in the field: "This compressor is short cycling and the high-pressure cutout is tripping — what are the most common causes?" The AI draws on service manuals, technical bulletins, and historical case data to give a structured troubleshooting path. This is not replacing the technician's judgment — it is augmenting it.

Video as the New Work Order

The paper service report is disappearing. In its place, forward-thinking field service organizations are building video-first documentation workflows where every site visit produces a structured video record. This shift has several important implications:

  • Dispute resolution becomes simple. When a customer claims the technician damaged something, or that a repair was not performed, the video record answers the question definitively. The cost savings on false warranty claims and liability disputes alone often justify the investment in video tools.
  • Customer trust increases. Customers who can see a video walkthrough of the work performed — including before-and-after footage of the equipment, the parts replaced, and the system test results — have dramatically higher satisfaction and confidence in the service. Sending a video summary after a visit has been shown to reduce callbacks and increase service contract renewal rates.
  • Training improves. Video documentation creates a library of real-world cases that can be used to train new technicians. A senior technician's approach to a complex diagnostic can be captured and shared in a way that written documentation never could.
  • AI can analyze the footage. Computer vision models can review video from a site visit and flag conditions the technician may have overlooked, verify that all steps in a service checklist were visually confirmed, or identify equipment outside the scope of the current work order that may need attention. This AI-assisted review layer is one of the most promising developments in field service technology.

Predictive Dispatch

This predictive model is better for customers (less downtime), better for technicians (less emergency-call pressure), and better for the business (higher margins on planned work versus reactive calls).

Tips for New Technicians

If you are early in your field service career, the advice that matters most is rarely found in a textbook. Here is what experienced technicians consistently wish they had known at the start:

Invest in Your Fundamentals

There is enormous temptation to specialize quickly — to become the go-to person for one brand or one system family. That specialization is valuable eventually, but it is best built on a solid foundation of electrical theory, mechanical principles, and diagnostic reasoning. Technicians who genuinely understand how things work can transfer their skills when equipment changes. Those who only know one system from memory are vulnerable when that system is replaced by a new generation of technology.

Document More Than You Think You Need To

New technicians consistently underestimate the value of documentation. The service report you file today protects you from a warranty dispute six months from now. The meter readings you record establish the baseline that makes a future diagnostic meaningful. The notes you leave in the system history help the next technician who serves that customer. Build documentation habits early — they compound over time.

Learn to Read Schematics

The single most universally valued technical skill across every field service discipline is the ability to read and trace an electrical schematic. Technicians who can look at a wiring diagram and mentally model how a circuit should behave can diagnose almost any electrical fault methodically. Those who cannot are left guessing. If your trade program did not emphasize schematic reading, invest in a good textbook or online course and practice until it becomes intuitive.

Treat Your Service Vehicle Like a Workspace

Your service van is your office, your workshop, and your storage room. A disorganized vehicle wastes time — not just for you, but for your customers who watch you dig through boxes looking for a part. Invest in a good shelving and bin system early. Keep your most frequently used tools in consistent locations so you can grab them without thinking. A clean, organized vehicle also makes a professional impression when a customer walks you out to your truck.

Master Your FSM Software

Many technicians treat their field service management app as a necessary inconvenience — something to fill out quickly before moving on. The best technicians treat it as a tool. The more complete and accurate your notes are in real time, the faster your billing gets processed, the fewer callbacks you receive, and the stronger the service history you leave behind for future visits. Take the time to learn every feature of the platform your company uses.

Learn to Explain Things Simply

The ability to explain a technical finding to a non-technical customer is a skill that separates good technicians from great ones. Practice translating what you find into plain language. Instead of "the compressor contactor is showing high resistance and the run capacitor is out of tolerance," try: "The part that starts your compressor is wearing out and one of the electrical components has degraded — we want to replace both now before you end up with a full system failure in July." Customers who understand what is wrong and why it matters make better decisions, are more likely to approve recommended repairs, and are more loyal over time.

Embrace Video Documentation from Day One

Technicians who develop strong video documentation habits early in their careers find that it becomes second nature — and that the benefits compound rapidly. Video protects you when customers have questions about what was done. It demonstrates your thoroughness to your manager. It builds customer trust. And as AI tools for analyzing field service video become more capable, technicians who are already capturing structured video will be ahead of those who are just learning to do so.

The technicians who thrive in 2026 are not just technically excellent — they are documented, communicative, and technology-fluent. The field service technician of the future is part engineer, part customer success manager, and part data collector.

The Field Service Technician in 2026 and Beyond

The field service technician role has never been more essential — or more technologically demanding. Across HVAC, medical equipment, telecommunications, utilities, and industrial automation, the professionals who travel to customer sites and keep critical systems running are the backbone of the service economy. They work independently, often under pressure, in conditions that no simulation can fully prepare you for.

What is changing is the layer of technology surrounding the work. AI-assisted diagnostics, IoT-driven predictive dispatch, AR-guided troubleshooting, and video-first documentation workflows are making field service technicians more capable and more accountable than any previous generation. The technicians who lean into these tools — who embrace documentation, master their platforms, and communicate with customers through video rather than just paper reports — are the ones building careers at the top of the salary range and advancing into leadership roles.

For organizations managing field service teams, the imperative is equally clear: the tools you give your technicians determine what they can accomplish. A team equipped with modern FSM software, IoT monitoring capabilities, and video documentation platforms will outperform a team without them on every metric that matters — first-time fix rate, customer satisfaction, warranty costs, and technician retention.
