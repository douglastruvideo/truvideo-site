MRO Software and Aviation Maintenance Technology: A 2026 Guide

Guide Aviation
TruVideo Team · April 2026 · 8 min read

In This Article

The MRO Software Landscape Core Capabilities of Modern MRO Platforms Regulatory Requirements Driving Technology Adoption Visual Inspection Technology in Aviation MRO AI Applications in Aircraft Maintenance Evaluating MRO Software Platforms The Future of Aviation Maintenance Technology

The global aviation MRO market exceeds $90 billion annually and is projected to grow significantly as fleet ages increase and air travel demand continues to recover and expand. Yet the technology infrastructure that supports aircraft maintenance, repair, and overhaul operations often lags a decade or more behind what's standard in other industrial sectors. Paper-based task cards, manual logbook entries, and disconnected inspection documentation remain common in shops maintaining some of the most complex machines on earth.

That's changing. The convergence of mobile technology, cloud computing, AI-powered analysis, and regulatory acceptance of electronic records is driving a fundamental modernization of MRO operations. This guide covers the current state of MRO software, what to look for in a platform, and how visual intelligence technology is creating new capabilities that didn't exist even two years ago.

The MRO Software Landscape

MRO software falls into three broad categories, each addressing a different layer of the maintenance operation.

MRO management platforms are the backbone systems that manage work orders, maintenance planning, parts inventory, and regulatory compliance documentation. These are the ERPs of aviation maintenance — platforms like AMOS, Ramco, IFS Maintenix, and Swiss AviationSoftware's AMOS that handle the operational orchestration of an MRO facility. They track what needs to be done, when, on which aircraft, with which parts, by which certified technicians.

Technical documentation systems manage the vast library of maintenance manuals, service bulletins, airworthiness directives, and task cards that govern every maintenance action. These systems ensure technicians have access to current documentation and that the facility can demonstrate compliance with manufacturer specifications and regulatory requirements.

Inspection and documentation tools handle the capture and analysis of inspection data — the visual evidence, measurements, and findings that result from physical examination of aircraft and components. This is the layer where the most significant innovation is happening, as mobile video, computer vision, and AI analysis transform what was historically a paper-and-pencil process into a structured data pipeline.

Core Capabilities of Modern MRO Platforms

Work package management. The ability to create, assign, track, and close complex work packages that include hundreds of individual task cards across multiple aircraft zones. Modern platforms handle task dependencies, parallel work streams, and automatic sequencing based on access requirements (you can't inspect internal structure until panels are removed).

Parts and inventory management. Aircraft parts management is uniquely complex. Every part has a trace history, a certification status, and life-limited tracking requirements. MRO software must manage parts from procurement through receipt, inspection, installation, and retirement — with full traceability at every step. Rotable part pooling, exchange programs, and repair-vs-replace decisions add layers that generic inventory systems can't handle.

Compliance tracking. Aviation maintenance is governed by a regulatory framework that requires documentation of every action, every part, and every person involved. MRO platforms must track airworthiness directive compliance, service bulletin incorporation, life-limited part status, and technician certification currency. The software doesn't just record compliance — it enforces it by preventing work assignment to uncertified personnel or installation of uncertified parts.

Planning and scheduling. Maintenance events range from overnight line checks to multi-week heavy checks that can involve hundreds of technicians. Planning tools must optimize hangar utilization, labor allocation, parts availability, and aircraft delivery schedules while accommodating the inevitable scope changes that emerge as inspections reveal additional required work.

Regulatory Requirements Driving Technology Adoption

Aviation regulators — the FAA, EASA, and their counterparts globally — have historically been conservative about technology adoption in maintenance operations. That conservatism is giving way to active encouragement of digital tools, driven by recognition that electronic records are more reliable, more searchable, and more auditable than paper.

FAA Advisory Circular 120-78A provides guidance on the use of electronic signatures, electronic recordkeeping, and electronic manuals in aviation maintenance. It explicitly permits electronic maintenance records provided they meet integrity, authentication, and security requirements. This circular removed the primary regulatory barrier to digital MRO systems.

EASA Part-145 and Part-M regulations have been updated to accommodate electronic maintenance management systems, with specific provisions for electronic work orders, digital task card sign-off, and electronic component tracking. EASA has been particularly progressive in recognizing AI-assisted inspection as a supplement to human inspection.

The regulatory direction is clear: electronic systems are not just permitted but increasingly expected. Facilities still running paper-based operations face growing scrutiny about data integrity, traceability, and the human error risks inherent in manual record-keeping.

Visual Inspection Technology in Aviation MRO

Aircraft inspection is fundamentally a visual process. Technicians examine surfaces for corrosion, cracks, delamination, fastener damage, and dozens of other defect types. They crawl into wheel wells, climb over wings, and inspect engine components through borescopes. The findings from these inspections drive the maintenance actions that keep aircraft safe and airworthy.

Historically, inspection findings were documented in text — a description of the defect, its location, and its severity, written on a task card or entered into a logbook. The problem with text-only documentation is that it's subjective, incomplete, and non-reproducible. Two technicians looking at the same corrosion can describe it differently. A text description can't capture the exact extent, color, or pattern of the finding. And there's no way to go back and re-examine the finding without re-accessing the aircraft.

Visual inspection technology — mobile video capture integrated with MRO workflow systems — addresses these limitations. When a technician records their inspection on video, the finding is captured with full visual context: exact location, extent, surrounding condition, and the inspector's assessment of severity. The video becomes a permanent, reviewable record that can be analyzed by remote experts, compared against future inspections, and processed by AI for automated defect detection.

For aviation MRO operations, the practical benefits include reduced aircraft-on-ground (AOG) time because remote engineers can evaluate findings from video rather than traveling to the facility, faster scope management during heavy checks because emerging findings are documented in real time, and improved quality assurance because supervisors can review inspection thoroughness from video records.

AI Applications in Aircraft Maintenance

AI in aviation MRO is moving from experimental to operational across several application areas.

Visual defect detection. Computer vision models trained on aircraft inspection imagery can detect and classify surface defects — corrosion, cracking, denting, paint deterioration, sealant failure — with accuracy approaching experienced inspectors. These models are most effective as a second pair of eyes, analyzing inspection video or photos to flag potential findings for human review rather than replacing the human inspector.

Borescope image analysis. Engine borescope inspections generate thousands of images per event. AI models can triage these images, flagging the ones that show potential defects (blade erosion, coating loss, thermal distress, foreign object damage) for specialist review. This reduces the time an engineer spends reviewing images from hours to minutes while maintaining the human decision-maker for disposition.

Predictive maintenance. By analyzing maintenance histories, operational data, and inspection trends across fleet populations, AI models can predict component failures before they occur. A turbine blade that shows a specific pattern of erosion across inspections might be flagged for preemptive replacement 500 flight hours before it would reach the threshold for in-service failure. The economic value of avoiding an unplanned engine removal is measured in hundreds of thousands of dollars per event.

Document analysis. Aviation maintenance generates enormous volumes of documentation — work orders, task cards, engineering orders, service bulletins, and airworthiness directives. AI-powered document analysis can cross-reference these documents to identify conflicting instructions, track compliance status across a fleet, and flag when a new service bulletin affects aircraft currently in maintenance.

Evaluating MRO Software Platforms

Selecting an MRO platform is a multi-year commitment with significant implementation costs. Here are the evaluation dimensions that matter most.

Regulatory compliance alignment. The platform must support your specific regulatory environment — FAA, EASA, TCCA, CAAC, or others. This includes not just record-keeping requirements but electronic signature standards, data retention policies, and audit trail specifications. Ask for specific references from operators under your regulator.

Integration architecture. MRO platforms don't operate in isolation. They need to integrate with airline planning systems, parts suppliers, customer portals, and — increasingly — visual inspection and AI analysis platforms. Evaluate the API architecture, webhook capabilities, and pre-built integrations. Closed platforms that require all functionality to live within their walls create bottlenecks as you adopt specialized tools for emerging capabilities like video inspection and AI analysis.

Mobile capability. Maintenance technicians work on aircraft, not at desks. The platform must function on tablets and phones in hangar environments — including areas with limited connectivity. Offline capability for task card completion, inspection documentation, and parts lookup is essential for heavy maintenance operations where Wi-Fi coverage is inconsistent.

Data ownership and portability. Aviation maintenance data has a lifecycle measured in decades — as long as the aircraft flies, its maintenance records must be maintained. Ensure your platform provides full data export in standard formats. Proprietary data formats that create vendor lock-in are particularly dangerous in aviation because the data has regulatory significance beyond your relationship with the software vendor.

Visual evidence integration. This is the emerging differentiator. Can the platform capture, store, and analyze visual inspection data — photos and video — as first-class data objects linked to work orders, task cards, and aircraft records? Or does it treat visual evidence as unstructured file attachments with no integration into the maintenance workflow? The platforms that treat visual data as structured intelligence rather than supplementary files will define the next generation of MRO technology.

The Future of Aviation Maintenance Technology

Three trends will shape MRO technology over the next five years.

Digital twins for maintenance. The concept of a digital twin — a virtual representation of the physical aircraft that updates in real time based on operational and maintenance data — is moving from concept to implementation. Visual inspection data is a critical input to the digital twin, providing the as-inspected condition that complements the as-designed specification. MRO platforms that can feed structured inspection data into digital twin systems will be at the center of this evolution.

Autonomous and semi-autonomous inspection. Drone-based inspection of aircraft exteriors and robotic inspection of confined spaces (fuel tanks, cargo compartments) are moving from proof of concept to early operational deployment. These systems generate massive volumes of visual data that must be processed, analyzed, and integrated into maintenance records. The capture technology is advancing rapidly; the bottleneck is the analysis and integration layer.

Federated AI across MRO networks. Individual MRO facilities don't generate enough inspection data to train specialized AI models for rare defect types. Federated learning — where models are trained across multiple facilities without sharing raw data — enables the industry to pool its collective inspection experience while maintaining data sovereignty. This is particularly relevant for the BYOS architecture approach, where each facility controls its data but contributes to shared model improvement.

Aviation maintenance is the most consequential inspection environment in the world. The technology that supports it must be worthy of that consequence — reliable, auditable, and built to standards that match the criticality of the work.
