The Ultimate Vehicle Inspection Checklist for 2026

Whether you run a dealership service lane, an independent repair shop, or a fleet maintenance operation, a thorough vehicle inspection checklist is the backbone of every service visit. It protects your customers, protects your business, and catches small problems before they become expensive failures.

This guide gives you a complete, system-by-system checklist you can reference or adapt for your own operation. We cover every major vehicle system, explain what to look for at each checkpoint, and share how modern digital vehicle inspection tools are making the entire process faster, more consistent, and more transparent.

Why You Need a Structured Vehicle Inspection Checklist

A standardized vehicle inspection form does more than satisfy compliance requirements. It creates a repeatable process that ensures nothing gets missed, regardless of which technician performs the work. Here is what a good checklist delivers:

  • Consistency — Every vehicle gets the same level of attention, every time.
  • Liability protection — A documented inspection report proves due diligence if a safety issue arises later.
  • Revenue discovery — Technicians who follow a structured checklist identify 30–50% more recommended services than those working from memory.
  • Customer trust — When you can show a customer exactly what was inspected and what was found, approval rates climb.
  • Training efficiency — New technicians ramp faster when they have a clear, step-by-step framework to follow.

The sections below organize a comprehensive multipoint inspection checklist by vehicle system. Use them as-is, or adapt them to match your shop's workflow and the vehicles you service most often.

Exterior Inspection Checklist

Start your walk-around at the front of the vehicle and move clockwise. The exterior inspection is often the first thing a customer sees in a completed inspection report, so thoroughness here builds immediate credibility.

Body and Paint

  • Check all body panels for dents, scratches, and rust spots
  • Inspect paint condition — fading, peeling, or clear coat failure
  • Look for misaligned panels that could indicate prior collision damage
  • Examine door edges, rocker panels, and wheel arches for corrosion
  • Check bumper covers for cracks, loose clips, or sagging

Glass and Mirrors

  • Inspect windshield for chips, cracks, and delamination
  • Check all side windows and rear glass for damage
  • Verify side mirrors are intact, adjustable, and properly secured
  • Test heated mirror function (if equipped)
  • Inspect wiper blades for cracking, streaking, or lifting

Lighting

  • Test headlights — low beam, high beam, and daytime running lights
  • Verify turn signals front and rear
  • Check brake lights, tail lights, and reverse lights
  • Inspect fog lights (if equipped)
  • Test hazard flashers
  • Check license plate lights
  • Look for moisture or condensation inside lens housings

Video-based inspections make the exterior walk-around far more powerful. Instead of writing "small chip in windshield," a technician can record a 10-second video that shows the customer exactly where the chip is, how large it is, and why it should be addressed before it spreads. That visual evidence consistently outperforms text-only findings when it comes to service approval rates.

Interior Inspection Checklist

The interior inspection covers both driver-facing controls and passenger comfort systems. It is also where you document mileage and identify the vehicle for your records.

  • Record odometer reading and VIN
  • Check dashboard warning lights — note any illuminated indicators
  • Test horn
  • Inspect seat belts for fraying, retraction, and latch function
  • Verify power seat adjustments (if equipped)
  • Test all power windows — up, down, and auto-reverse
  • Check power locks and child safety locks
  • Inspect steering wheel for play, cracks, or worn leather
  • Test climate control — heat, A/C, fan speeds, and mode selection
  • Check cabin air filter condition
  • Inspect pedal pads for wear
  • Test parking brake engagement and release
  • Check rearview mirror and auto-dimming function
  • Inspect floor mats for secure attachment (no pedal interference)
  • Verify infotainment system powers on, Bluetooth pairs, and backup camera displays

Under-the-Hood Inspection Checklist

The engine compartment inspection is where many of the highest-value findings live. A systematic approach here ensures you do not overlook slow leaks, worn belts, or corroded connections.

Engine and Drivetrain

  • Visually inspect engine for oil leaks, coolant leaks, and seepage
  • Check serpentine belt for cracks, glazing, fraying, or excessive slack
  • Inspect timing belt/chain cover for leaks (if applicable)
  • Check accessory drive belt tensioner
  • Inspect motor mounts and transmission mounts for cracking or collapse
  • Look for rodent damage to wiring harnesses and hoses
  • Check PCV valve and associated hoses

Hoses and Connections

  • Squeeze radiator hoses — check for soft spots, bulges, or hardening
  • Inspect heater hoses for leaks or deterioration
  • Check all clamp connections for tightness
  • Inspect vacuum lines for cracks or disconnection
  • Verify fuel line condition and connections

Air Intake and Filtration

  • Remove and inspect engine air filter — note contamination level
  • Check air filter housing for proper sealing
  • Inspect intake ductwork for cracks or loose connections
  • Verify mass airflow sensor (MAF) is clean and connected

This is an area where digital inspection tools provide an outsized advantage. A photo of a dirty air filter next to a clean one is worth a thousand words on a paper inspection form. Shops that adopt visual inspection reporting frequently see their air filter and belt replacement rates increase significantly — not because they are overselling, but because customers can finally see the evidence for themselves.

Under-Vehicle Inspection Checklist

With the vehicle on a lift, the technician gains access to components that are invisible from above. This section of the checklist tends to surface the most safety-critical findings.

  • Inspect exhaust system — manifold, catalytic converter, muffler, pipes, and hangers
  • Check for exhaust leaks (visual and auditory)
  • Inspect CV joints and boots for tears or grease leakage
  • Check U-joints (rear-wheel and all-wheel drive) for play
  • Inspect driveshaft for damage or imbalance
  • Check differential housing for leaks
  • Inspect transfer case (if equipped) for leaks
  • Look at transmission pan for seepage or damage
  • Inspect oil pan and drain plug for leaks
  • Check frame, subframe, and crossmembers for rust, cracks, or collision damage
  • Inspect suspension components — control arms, bushings, ball joints, and sway bar links
  • Check steering components — tie rod ends, rack boots, and power steering lines
  • Inspect fuel tank and lines for damage or corrosion
  • Check heat shields for rattles or missing hardware

Brake System Inspection Checklist

Brakes are the most safety-critical system on any vehicle. Your vehicle inspection checklist should measure and document every wear component, not just pass or fail them.

Front Brakes

  • Measure front brake pad thickness (record in mm or percentage remaining)
  • Inspect front rotors for scoring, grooves, heat spots, and minimum thickness
  • Check front calipers for leaks, seized slides, and even pad wear
  • Inspect front brake lines and hoses for cracks, bulging, or corrosion

Rear Brakes

  • Measure rear brake pad or shoe thickness
  • Inspect rear rotors or drums for wear and damage
  • Check rear calipers or wheel cylinders for leaks
  • Inspect rear brake lines and hoses
  • Check parking brake cable adjustment and condition

Brake Hydraulics

  • Check brake fluid level and condition (clear, amber, or dark)
  • Test brake pedal feel — firm, spongy, or sinking
  • Inspect master cylinder for leaks
  • Check ABS module and sensors for codes or physical damage
  • Verify brake warning light operation

Brake measurements are one of the most persuasive things you can communicate visually. When a technician records a video showing a caliper with 2mm of pad remaining next to a new pad, the customer does not need to take anyone's word for it. Digital inspection platforms like TruVideo Inspect make it easy to capture these measurements on video and send them directly to the vehicle owner for review and approval.

Tire and Wheel Inspection Checklist

Tires are the only part of the vehicle that contacts the road, making them critical to safety, handling, and fuel economy. Document measurements for all four tires plus the spare.

  • Measure tread depth at three points across each tire (inside, center, outside) — record in 32nds of an inch or mm
  • Check for uneven wear patterns indicating alignment or suspension issues
  • Inspect sidewalls for bulges, cuts, dry rot, or cracking
  • Verify tire age using DOT date code (tires older than 6 years should be flagged)
  • Check tire pressure against door placard specification
  • Inspect valve stems and caps for damage or leaking
  • Check wheel condition — curb rash, cracks, or bends
  • Verify lug nut torque and condition
  • Inspect spare tire pressure and condition
  • Check TPMS sensor function (verify no warning light)

Tire wear patterns tell a story. A multipoint inspection that documents inner-edge wear, for example, is a natural entry point for an alignment conversation. When you can send the customer a photo or video of that wear pattern alongside a clear explanation, the service writes itself.

Fluids Inspection Checklist

Fluid condition and levels are among the easiest items to check and the most commonly neglected by vehicle owners. This section of the checklist should document both level and condition for every fluid.

  • Engine oil — Check level and condition. Note color (golden, brown, black) and any signs of contamination (milky = coolant intrusion, metallic particles = internal wear).
  • Coolant/antifreeze — Check level in reservoir and radiator (when cold). Test concentration with a refractometer. Note color and look for rust or sediment.
  • Transmission fluid — Check level (per manufacturer procedure). Note color and smell. Burnt smell or dark color indicates degradation.
  • Brake fluid — Check level against min/max marks. Test moisture content with a brake fluid tester. Fluid should be clear to light amber.
  • Power steering fluid — Check level and condition. Note any foaming (indicates air in system).
  • Windshield washer fluid — Check level and top off.
  • Differential fluid (front and rear, if applicable) — Check level and note any metallic particles or burnt smell.
  • Transfer case fluid (if equipped) — Check level and condition.

Fluid checks are another area where a visual record is invaluable. Photographing transmission fluid on a white paper towel to show its color compared to fresh fluid makes the case for a fluid exchange far more effectively than a line on a paper vehicle inspection form that reads "transmission fluid: dark."

Electrical System Inspection Checklist

Modern vehicles contain dozens of electronic modules and miles of wiring. While a full electrical diagnosis is beyond a standard inspection, these checks catch the most common issues.

  • Battery — Test voltage (should be 12.4V+ with engine off). Perform load test or conductance test. Inspect terminals for corrosion. Check hold-down bracket.
  • Charging system — Test alternator output (should be 13.5–14.8V at idle). Check belt condition and tension.
  • Starting system — Note cranking speed. Check starter draw if slow crank is reported.
  • Test all exterior lights (covered in exterior section)
  • Test interior lights — dome, map, courtesy, and trunk
  • Verify instrument cluster — all gauges functional, no warning lights
  • Check power outlet and USB port function
  • Test keyless entry and push-button start
  • Scan for diagnostic trouble codes (DTCs) with an OBD-II scanner
  • Check for open recalls using VIN lookup

A battery test result printed from a conductance tester is good. A video showing the tester connected to the battery, the test running, and the result displaying on screen is better. It eliminates any question about whether the test was actually performed on that specific vehicle. Platforms built for automotive service operations make this kind of visual documentation a natural part of the workflow rather than an extra step.

Safety and Emissions Inspection Checklist

Many states and municipalities require periodic safety or emissions inspections. Even where not legally mandated, these checks protect your shop from liability and protect your customers on the road.

Safety Items

  • Verify all seat belts latch, retract, and are free of damage
  • Check airbag warning light — should illuminate briefly at startup then extinguish
  • Inspect windshield wipers and washers for function
  • Verify horn operation
  • Check all mirrors for proper adjustment and condition
  • Inspect door latches and hinges
  • Verify child safety lock function (rear doors)
  • Check hood latch and safety catch
  • Inspect jack and lug wrench presence and condition

Emissions Items

  • Check for illuminated MIL (malfunction indicator lamp / check engine light)
  • Verify catalytic converter is present and not damaged
  • Inspect EGR system for proper function (if applicable)
  • Check EVAP system — gas cap seal, purge valve, and charcoal canister
  • Verify PCV system is functional
  • Check exhaust for visible smoke or excessive odor
  • Run OBD-II readiness monitors to confirm all have completed

Digital vs. Paper Vehicle Inspection Forms

For decades, the standard vehicle inspection form was a paper tri-fold with checkboxes. It worked — sort of. But paper forms introduce problems that compound over time:

  • Illegibility — Handwritten notes in a service bay are rarely clear enough for office staff or customers to read.
  • Lost documentation — Paper gets misplaced, coffee-stained, or thrown away. When a customer calls three months later asking about a recommendation, good luck finding it.
  • Zero visual proof — A checkbox that says "brakes: needs attention" does not tell the customer anything. Was it 4mm? 2mm? Metal on metal?
  • No tracking — Paper forms make it nearly impossible to track inspection completion rates, technician consistency, or service recommendation trends across your operation.
  • Slow delivery — The customer has to be physically present, or the advisor has to call and describe findings verbally.

Digital inspection platforms solve all of these problems. A modern digital vehicle inspection workflow lets technicians capture photos and video alongside each checklist item, automatically generates a professional inspection report, and sends it to the customer via text or email within minutes.

The result is faster approvals, higher average repair orders, and dramatically better customer retention. Shops that switch from paper to digital inspection tools consistently report a 20–30% increase in service revenue, not from upselling but from actually communicating findings in a way customers can understand and act on.

The best inspection is the one the customer actually sees and understands. Paper checklists rarely leave the service counter. A video inspection sent to a customer's phone gets watched within minutes.

Pre-Purchase Inspection Checklist

A pre-purchase inspection checklist covers everything above plus additional items specific to evaluating a vehicle before buying it. If your shop performs PPIs, add these to your standard inspection form:

  • Pull vehicle history report (accident, title, service records)
  • Verify VIN matches title, door sticker, and dashboard
  • Check paint depth readings across all panels (detects repainting after collision)
  • Inspect door jambs and trunk seams for overspray or mismatched paint
  • Look for welding marks on structural components
  • Check frame and unibody for straightening evidence
  • Road test: evaluate engine performance, transmission shifting, brake feel, steering alignment, and suspension behavior
  • Listen for unusual noises — engine, drivetrain, and suspension
  • Check all electrical accessories and convenience features
  • Test A/C output temperature
  • Perform comprehensive OBD-II scan including manufacturer-specific codes
  • Check for open recalls
  • Document overall condition with photos/video for the buyer's records

Pre-purchase inspections are an excellent use case for video-based inspection tools. The buyer may not be present at your shop, so sending them a detailed video walk-through of every finding — positive and negative — builds enormous trust in your assessment. It also creates a permanent record that protects both the buyer and your shop.

Using This Checklist in Your Shop

A checklist is only useful if it is actually used, consistently, on every vehicle. Here are practical steps to turn this reference into a working process:

  1. Customize for your operation. Not every shop services every type of vehicle. Trim this list to match your core work, and add specialized items for your niche (diesel, hybrid/EV, heavy truck, fleet).
  2. Standardize the workflow. Define when the inspection happens in your service process, who performs it, and how findings get communicated to the advisor and the customer.
  3. Go digital. If you are still using paper forms, the single highest-impact change you can make is switching to a digital inspection platform. The combination of structured checklists, photo/video documentation, and instant customer delivery transforms the inspection from a compliance exercise into a revenue and trust engine.
  4. Measure and improve. Track inspection completion rates by technician. Track average findings per inspection. Track customer approval rates by finding type. Use this data to coach, improve, and optimize.
  5. Train continuously. Review inspections as a team. Highlight great examples. Discuss findings that were missed. Build a culture where thoroughness is the standard, not the exception.
