Aircraft Inspection Documentation Software: How Video Is Replacing Paper in MRO

Guide Aviation
TruVideo Team · April 2026 · 10 min read

In This Article

Every aircraft inspection generates documentation. Every bolt torqued, every panel opened, every finding recorded. For decades, that documentation lived on paper task cards, handwritten logbook entries, and photographs stapled to manila folders. The system worked — in the sense that it satisfied regulatory requirements and kept aircraft flying safely. But it was slow, fragile, and fundamentally disconnected from the digital systems that run modern MRO operations.

That era is ending. Aircraft inspection documentation software is shifting from static records to dynamic, video-based evidence that can be searched, analyzed, and audited at a level paper never allowed. The catalyst isn't just efficiency — it's the recognition that video provides a fundamentally better evidentiary record than any written description or still photograph ever could.

The Documentation Crisis in Aviation MRO

The scale of aviation maintenance documentation is staggering. A single C-check on a narrow-body aircraft generates thousands of individual task card sign-offs, hundreds of inspection findings, and dozens of non-routine work orders. A heavy maintenance visit on a wide-body can produce documentation measured in linear feet of paper when stacked.

The problem isn't volume alone — it's the gap between what was actually observed during an inspection and what the documentation captures. A technician performing a detailed visual inspection of a wing spar might spend forty-five minutes examining surface conditions, checking for corrosion, fatigue cracking, and delamination. The resulting documentation? A checkbox marked "satisfactory" and perhaps a three-sentence narrative if something was found. The vast majority of what the technician observed — the context, the comparison points, the areas of concern that didn't quite rise to a finding — is lost.

This documentation gap creates three problems. First, it makes quality assurance reviews dependent on trusting the inspector's judgment without being able to independently verify what was observed. Second, it makes trend analysis nearly impossible because there's no baseline visual record to compare against future inspections. Third, it creates vulnerability during regulatory audits, where the burden of proof falls on the MRO to demonstrate that inspections were thorough and complete.

FAA and EASA Documentation Requirements

FAA requirements. Under 14 CFR Part 43, every person performing maintenance, preventive maintenance, or alterations must make an entry in the aircraft's maintenance record. Part 145 repair stations must maintain records that document the work performed, the methods used, and the approval for return to service. The FAA doesn't prescribe the format of these records — paper, electronic, or video are all acceptable — but they must be complete, accurate, and retrievable.

EASA requirements. EASA Part 145 is more prescriptive. AMO (Approved Maintenance Organisation) certificate holders must maintain a work order system that records the details of all maintenance performed. EASA's emphasis on "continued airworthiness" requires documentation that demonstrates not just that maintenance was performed, but that it was performed correctly and completely. The 2024 updates to EASA Part-CAMO (Continuing Airworthiness Management Organisation) regulations explicitly encourage electronic record-keeping systems that provide enhanced traceability.

ICAO Annex 6 and 8. The International Civil Aviation Organization sets the global framework that national regulators build upon. ICAO standards require that maintenance records be "kept in a form acceptable to the State" and that they include sufficient detail to determine the airworthiness status of the aircraft. The trend across ICAO member states is toward accepting — and in some cases preferring — electronic documentation that provides richer evidence than traditional paper records.

The regulatory environment is moving decisively in favor of digital documentation. No major aviation regulator prohibits electronic inspection records, and several actively incentivize them through reduced audit burden and streamlined approval processes for operators that demonstrate robust digital documentation systems.

Traditional vs. Digital Inspection Records

Paper-based documentation has been the standard for seventy years. Task cards are printed, distributed to technicians, completed by hand, collected by supervisors, reviewed by quality assurance, and filed in physical archives. The process is linear, sequential, and entirely dependent on human legibility and diligence. A smudged signature, an illegible finding description, or a misfiled task card can create compliance gaps that aren't discovered until an audit — sometimes years later.

Photo-based digital documentation was the first wave of modernization. Technicians capture still photos with tablets or smartphones, attach them to electronic work orders, and file them in document management systems. This was a significant improvement over paper — photos don't have handwriting legibility issues, they can be tagged and searched, and they provide visual evidence that supplements written descriptions. But photographs have fundamental limitations as inspection evidence: they lack temporal context, they can't capture motion or process, and a single photo shows a single moment from a single angle.

Video-based documentation represents the current frontier. A continuous video record of an inspection captures everything the inspector observed — the approach, the inspection sequence, the areas of concern, and the overall condition — in a single artifact that provides orders of magnitude more information than any number of photographs. Video documentation doesn't replace the written record; it supplements it with an evidentiary layer that makes the written record verifiable and auditable.

Non-Destructive Testing Documentation

Non-destructive testing (NDT) is where documentation requirements are most demanding and where video provides the greatest advantage over traditional methods. NDT techniques — ultrasonic testing, eddy current inspection, radiographic testing, thermography, and visual inspection with borescopes — generate data that must be recorded, interpreted, and archived with precision.

Ultrasonic testing produces waveform data that indicates material thickness and internal defect characteristics. Traditional documentation requires the technician to record equipment settings, calibration data, scan patterns, and interpretation results. Video documentation of the ultrasonic display during scanning captures all of this in real time, including the waveform patterns that experienced technicians use to distinguish between acceptable variations and genuine defects.

Eddy current inspection is used extensively for detecting fatigue cracks around fastener holes and in structural members. The impedance plane display that technicians interpret is inherently temporal — crack indications appear as the probe moves across the test area. A still photograph of an impedance plane display captures a single moment; a video captures the entire scan sequence, showing how indications develop and whether they're consistent with crack signatures or benign artifacts.

Borescope inspection is perhaps the most natural fit for video documentation. Technicians already use video-capable borescopes to inspect engine internals, fuel tanks, and other confined spaces. The transition from "video for real-time viewing" to "video as permanent inspection record" requires only workflow changes, not equipment changes. The result is a complete visual record of every bore that was inspected, every surface that was examined, and every finding that was identified — far richer than the still-frame captures that most borescope inspection reports currently include.

How Video Provides Frame-by-Frame Evidence

The evidentiary value of video in aircraft inspection documentation comes from three properties that photographs and written descriptions lack.

Temporal continuity. A video captures the entire inspection sequence, not just the moments a technician chose to photograph. This means that a reviewer — whether quality assurance, a regulatory auditor, or a future maintenance planner — can see not only what was found, but what the inspector looked at and didn't find. The absence of findings is as important as their presence in aviation maintenance, and only video captures both.

Timestamped frames. Modern aircraft inspection documentation software embeds precise timestamps into every frame of inspection video. This creates an immutable timeline that shows exactly when each area was inspected, how long the technician spent on each zone, and the sequence in which the inspection was performed. If a question arises during an audit — "Was the aft spar inspected before or after the corrosion treatment?" — the timestamp record provides an unambiguous answer.

Frame-by-frame analysis. Individual video frames can be extracted, annotated, measured, and analyzed with the same precision as still photographs, but with the added context of the frames that precede and follow them. This is particularly valuable for documenting conditions that exist at the boundary between serviceable and unserviceable — a corrosion pit that's close to the damage limit, a crack that might be a scratch, a surface condition that requires experienced judgment. Frame-by-frame review allows multiple experts to evaluate the same evidence independently, without the interpretation loss that occurs when one technician describes a finding in writing for another to assess.

Integration with MRO Systems

Aircraft inspection documentation software is only useful if it integrates with the MRO management platforms that orchestrate maintenance operations. Video inspection records that live in a disconnected system — a separate app, a shared drive, an email attachment — lose most of their value because they're not linked to the work orders, task cards, and aircraft records they document.

AMOS integration. Swiss AviationSoftware's AMOS is the most widely deployed MRO management platform globally. Effective integration means video inspection records are linked directly to AMOS task cards and work orders, visible within the AMOS interface, and included in the documentation package that AMOS generates for return-to-service approval. The integration should be bidirectional: AMOS pushes task card requirements to the inspection documentation tool, and the tool pushes completed inspection videos back to AMOS with structured metadata.

Ramco integration. Ramco Aviation Suite serves a growing segment of MROs, particularly in Asia-Pacific and the Middle East. Integration with Ramco follows similar patterns — linking video evidence to work packages, populating inspection findings with visual references, and ensuring that Ramco's compliance tracking system recognizes video documentation as satisfying inspection record requirements.

SAP MRO integration. SAP's aviation maintenance module is used by many airline-owned MRO operations and large independent providers. SAP's enterprise architecture makes integration more complex but also more powerful — video inspection data can flow through SAP's document management system, link to equipment master records, and feed into SAP's analytics and reporting infrastructure.

The key requirement across all integrations is that video becomes a first-class data object in the MRO system, not a file attachment. It should be searchable by aircraft registration, ATA chapter, work order number, and finding type. It should appear in the context where technicians and inspectors work, not in a separate application they have to remember to check.

AI for Detecting Corrosion, Cracks, and Delamination

The combination of video inspection records and AI analysis creates capabilities that didn't exist in the paper-based world. When every inspection is documented on video, AI models can process that video to detect, classify, and measure defects with consistency that supplements human judgment.

Corrosion detection. Surface corrosion, pitting, intergranular corrosion, and exfoliation each have distinct visual signatures that AI models can be trained to identify. TruVideo's AI, trained on a dataset of over 80 million inspection videos across multiple industries, can flag potential corrosion in inspection footage and classify it by type and severity. This doesn't replace the technician's judgment — it provides a second set of eyes that never gets fatigued, never rushes through the last inspection of a twelve-hour shift, and never overlooks an area because it was partially obstructed.

Crack detection. Fatigue cracks in metallic structure often start as barely visible surface indications that grow over time. AI-assisted video analysis can detect linear indications that might be cracks, measure their apparent length, and — critically — compare them against previous inspection videos of the same area to determine whether an indication is new or has been growing. This temporal comparison capability, where AI compares the same structural area across inspections performed months or years apart, is one of the most powerful applications of video-based documentation.

Composite delamination. Composite structures present unique inspection challenges because damage is often subsurface and not visible to the naked eye. However, surface anomalies — paint cracking, slight surface distortion, discoloration around impact areas — can indicate underlying delamination. AI trained on large datasets of composite damage can identify these subtle surface indicators in inspection video, flagging areas for further investigation with ultrasonic or thermographic NDT methods.

AI doesn't replace the inspector. It gives the inspector a colleague that has seen eighty million inspections and never forgets what a growing crack looks like.

The Compliance Audit Trail

Regulatory audits in aviation maintenance are fundamentally about one question: can you prove that the work was performed correctly? Paper-based documentation answers this question with signatures and written descriptions. Video-based documentation answers it with visual evidence that shows exactly what was done, when, and by whom.

Chain of custody. Aircraft inspection documentation software maintains a complete chain of custody for every video record. The system logs who captured the video, when it was captured, which aircraft and zone it documents, when it was reviewed, who reviewed it, and whether any annotations or findings were added. This chain of custody is cryptographically signed and tamper-evident — any modification to a video record or its metadata is logged and visible to auditors.

Retention and retrieval. Aviation maintenance records must be retained for specific periods depending on the type of work — typically two years for routine maintenance and the life of the aircraft for major structural repairs. Video-based documentation systems handle retention automatically, applying retention policies based on work order type and regulatory jurisdiction. When an auditor requests documentation for a specific inspection, the system retrieves the complete package — video, metadata, annotations, findings, and sign-offs — in seconds rather than the hours or days it can take to locate paper records in physical archives.

Audit preparation. MRO facilities spend significant resources preparing for regulatory audits — pulling records, organizing documentation, creating audit packages. Video-based documentation reduces this burden dramatically because the records are already organized, indexed, and searchable. An auditor can search for all inspections of a specific ATA chapter, across all aircraft, within a date range, and review the video evidence directly — without relying on the MRO to assemble and present the documentation.

Remote Expert Review with Smart Glasses

Some of the most critical inspection decisions in aviation maintenance require expert judgment that may not be available on-site. A corrosion finding that's borderline between serviceable and requiring repair. A crack indication that needs interpretation by a Level III NDT technician. A structural condition that the aircraft manufacturer's engineering team needs to assess before the MRO can proceed.

Traditionally, these situations result in delays. The finding is documented with photos and a written description, sent to the expert via email, and the work stops until the expert responds — which might take hours or days depending on availability and time zones. In some cases, the expert travels to the facility to see the finding in person, adding days and significant cost to the maintenance event.

TruLabs smart glasses change this equation. A technician wearing TruLabs glasses can stream live video of an inspection finding to a remote expert, who sees exactly what the technician sees — in real time, with the ability to annotate the technician's field of view, request different angles, and guide additional examination. The expert's assessment is made with the same visual evidence they'd have if they were standing next to the aircraft, and the entire session is recorded as part of the permanent inspection record.

The applications extend beyond ad-hoc expert consultations:

  • OEM engineering support. Aircraft manufacturers can review structural findings in real time during heavy maintenance, providing engineering dispositions without travel or delay.
  • Regulatory inspector oversight. Designated Engineering Representatives (DERs) and Authorized Inspectors can observe critical inspections remotely, reducing scheduling constraints and travel costs while maintaining oversight quality.
  • Training and mentorship. Junior technicians can receive real-time guidance from experienced inspectors during complex inspections, with the session recorded as both a training record and an inspection document.
  • Multi-facility collaboration. MRO networks can share expertise across facilities without relocating personnel, ensuring that specialized knowledge is available wherever it's needed.

The combination of hands-free video capture, live streaming, AI-assisted analysis, and permanent archival creates an inspection documentation workflow that is simultaneously more thorough, more efficient, and more auditable than anything paper-based systems could achieve.

The question is no longer whether aviation MRO will go digital. It's whether your facility will lead the transition or be forced into it by competitors and regulators who already have.
