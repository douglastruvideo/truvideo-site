The Multipoint Inspection Guide: How Dealerships Turn Inspections into Revenue

Guide Automotive
TruVideo Team · April 2026 · 7 min read

In This Article

Every vehicle that enters your service drive represents two opportunities: the work the customer came in for, and the work they don't yet know they need. The multipoint inspection is the process that bridges those two opportunities. When done well, it protects the customer from unexpected breakdowns and generates the incremental revenue that makes fixed operations profitable. When done poorly, it's a wasted 10 minutes that produces a form nobody reads.

The difference between a multipoint inspection that generates $200 in additional approved work and one that generates nothing isn't the checklist — it's how findings are documented, communicated, and presented. This guide covers the complete MPI process, from what to inspect through how to present findings in a way that earns customer trust and approval.

What Is a Multipoint Inspection?

A multipoint inspection (MPI) is a comprehensive vehicle health check performed during a service visit, covering major vehicle systems regardless of what the customer originally brought the car in for. If a customer comes in for an oil change, the MPI might reveal that their brake pads are at 3mm, their cabin air filter is clogged, and their serpentine belt is showing cracks.

Most OEMs have a defined MPI checklist that their franchise dealerships are expected to follow. Toyota, Honda, Ford, GM, and others each have their own inspection forms with specific items and grading criteria. Independent shops typically use industry-standard checklists or develop their own based on the vehicle systems they're equipped to service.

The MPI is distinct from a digital vehicle inspection in emphasis rather than substance. While DVI is a technology category describing the tools used to perform and document inspections, the MPI is a service process — a specific set of checks performed at a specific time (during a service visit) for a specific purpose (identifying additional needed work). In practice, the best MPIs use DVI technology, but not all DVIs are MPIs.

The Standard Inspection Points

While OEM-specific checklists vary, a comprehensive multipoint inspection covers these systems. Most dealerships inspect between 27 and 35 individual items.

Under the hood

Engine oil level and condition, transmission fluid, brake fluid, power steering fluid, coolant level and condition, windshield washer fluid, battery terminals and charge level, belts and hoses, air filter, and engine bay leaks. Each fluid check should note not just the level but the condition — dark or gritty transmission fluid tells a different story than fluid that's simply low.

Brakes

Front and rear brake pad thickness (measured in millimeters), rotor condition (look for scoring, heat spots, and lip wear), brake line condition, caliper function, parking brake operation, and brake fluid quality (test with a moisture meter if available). Brakes are the highest-value MPI finding category — they represent both a significant safety item and a substantial revenue opportunity.

Tires and wheels

Tread depth at multiple points across each tire (measured in 32nds of an inch), tire pressure, sidewall condition, uneven wear patterns that suggest alignment issues, wheel condition, lug nut torque, and spare tire presence and condition. Tire findings are the second most common MPI revenue category after brakes.

Steering and suspension

Ball joints, tie rod ends, control arm bushings, struts/shocks (look for leaks and bounce test results), CV joint boots, wheel bearing play, and power steering system. Suspension findings are harder to communicate to customers because the wear is gradual and doesn't produce obvious symptoms until it's severe.

Exterior and lighting

All exterior lights (headlights, tail lights, brake lights, turn signals, markers), windshield condition, wiper blade condition, body damage, and glass chips. Lighting issues are low-cost, high-approval-rate findings that demonstrate thoroughness.

Interior and HVAC

Cabin air filter, HVAC operation, seat belt function, instrument cluster warning lights, and any interior odors that suggest coolant leaks, electrical issues, or HVAC mold.

The Red-Yellow-Green Framework

The industry-standard approach to MPI findings uses a three-tier severity classification.

Red — requires immediate attention. Safety-critical items that should be addressed before the vehicle leaves the shop. Examples: brake pads below 2mm, tire tread below 2/32", significant fluid leaks, non-functional brake lights. Red findings carry the highest customer approval rates because the safety argument is clear and immediate.

Yellow — needs attention soon. Items that are approaching the end of their service life but aren't yet safety-critical. Examples: brake pads between 2-4mm, tires with uneven wear but adequate remaining tread, aging serpentine belt with surface cracks, coolant showing early contamination. Yellow findings are the MPI revenue battleground — the difference between a high-performing and low-performing service department is the yellow approval rate.

Green — good condition. Items that passed inspection. Documenting green findings matters because it builds trust. When a customer sees that the technician checked 32 items and only flagged three as yellow, the yellow findings carry more credibility. If you only report problems, customers suspect you're looking for problems.

Presenting Findings That Get Approved

The inspection itself is only half the equation. The other half is how findings reach the customer and how the approval conversation is handled.

Lead with the green. Start the customer conversation with what's in good shape. This establishes that you performed a thorough inspection and that most of their vehicle is healthy. It frames the yellow and red findings as specific exceptions, not a general upsell pitch.

Show, don't tell. This is where the transition from paper MPI to digital vehicle inspection technology pays for itself. Telling a customer "your brake pads are at 3mm" is abstract. Showing them a video of the technician measuring the pads, pointing to the wear indicator, and explaining that 3mm means approximately 2-3 months of remaining life at their driving patterns — that's a conversation that leads to approval. Visual evidence transforms the dynamic from "the advisor says I need brakes" to "I can see I need brakes."

Prioritize and phase. Not every customer can approve $1,500 in found work on the spot. A skilled advisor prioritizes findings by urgency and presents phased options: "The brakes are the priority today — that's $450. The serpentine belt and cabin filter can wait until your next visit in three months. I'll make a note so we catch them then." This approach builds trust, increases today's approval rate, and creates a future appointment.

Make approval frictionless. The best communication platforms let customers review findings and approve work from their phone without calling the dealership. Text a link to the inspection results, let the customer watch the video at their own pace, and provide a one-tap approval mechanism. Every phone call you eliminate from the approval process reduces time-to-approval and increases the approval rate.

Common MPI Mistakes That Kill Revenue

Inconsistent inspection thoroughness. When some technicians inspect 32 items and others inspect 15, your data becomes unreliable and your customer experience is inconsistent. Standardize the process, enforce the checklist, and hold every tech to the same standard. Technology helps here — digital inspection platforms can enforce mandatory fields and flag incomplete inspections before they reach the advisor.

Skipping the MPI on quick services. Shops that skip the multipoint on express services (oil changes, tire rotations) are leaving the highest-volume inspection opportunity on the table. Quick service customers are in your shop more frequently and represent the easiest path to incremental revenue. The MPI on a quick service should be streamlined but never skipped.

Presenting findings as a wall of text. A paper printout or dense email with 30 line items is overwhelming. Customers shut down. Prioritize, categorize, and present findings visually. The red-yellow-green framework exists specifically to prevent information overload.

Failing to track declined work. When a customer declines a yellow finding, that's not the end of the conversation — it's the beginning. Track declined items, set follow-up reminders, and reference the previous inspection at the next visit. Declined work should be systematically followed up, not forgotten.

Video-Enhanced Multipoint Inspections

The shift from paper-based MPI to video-enhanced MPI is the single highest-ROI technology investment a service department can make. The core insight is simple: customers approve work they can see and understand. Video provides both.

A video MPI follows the same inspection structure as a paper MPI, but the technician records a walkthrough video during the inspection rather than (or in addition to) checking boxes. The video captures the technician's perspective — what they see, what they measure, and why it matters. This video is then sent to the customer, typically via text message, where they can review it on their own time. For a deep dive on presentation strategy, see our guide on how video increases repair order revenue.

The results are consistent across dealers who make this transition. Customer pay repair order values increase 20-30%. Approval rates on yellow findings roughly double. Customer satisfaction scores improve because the process feels transparent rather than adversarial. And CSI complaints about "unnecessary upselling" drop because the customer can see the evidence themselves.

AI-Assisted Inspections: The Next Level

The next evolution of the multipoint inspection adds artificial intelligence to the process. AI-assisted MPI uses computer vision to analyze inspection video and photos, automatically detecting and classifying findings that supplement the technician's observations.

The most immediate application is damage detection — AI models trained on thousands of brake pad images can estimate remaining pad life from a photo with accuracy comparable to physical measurement. Tire tread analysis, belt condition assessment, and fluid color analysis are all increasingly automated. This doesn't replace the technician. It augments them — catching findings they might miss on a busy day and standardizing the severity classification so "yellow" means the same thing across every technician and every location.

For multi-rooftop dealer groups, AI-assisted MPI also enables fleet-level analytics. Which vehicle models develop brake wear fastest? What's the average mileage at which CV boots start failing in your climate? Are certain technicians consistently finding more or fewer issues than their peers? These insights were impossible with paper-based inspections and difficult even with basic digital checklists. With AI analyzing structured data from every inspection, they become routine.

The multipoint inspection isn't just a revenue tool — it's the foundation of a data-driven service operation. Every inspection generates intelligence about vehicle health, customer behavior, and operational efficiency. The dealers who capture and act on that intelligence are the ones who will lead their markets.
