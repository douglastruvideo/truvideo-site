7 Best Loom Alternatives for Engineering Teams in 2026

Loom defined the async video category. For years, it was the default screen recording tool for remote teams — simple, fast, and good enough for most use cases. Then Atlassian acquired it, and the landscape shifted. Pricing changed. The product roadmap realigned toward Atlassian's enterprise suite. And engineering teams — the power users who built their code review, bug reporting, and documentation workflows around Loom — started asking whether there was something purpose-built for how they actually work.

The answer is yes. The async video market in 2026 is more competitive and more specialized than ever. This guide evaluates seven Loom alternatives specifically through the lens of engineering team requirements: data sovereignty, self-hosting options, API-first architecture, developer tool integrations, and the workflows that matter for shipping software.

Why Engineering Teams Are Looking Beyond Loom

The post-acquisition Loom works fine for sales teams recording product demos and managers sending update videos. But engineering teams have requirements that general-purpose async video tools don't address well.

Data sovereignty matters to engineering organizations. When you're recording code reviews that show proprietary source code, architecture walkthroughs that reveal system design, or incident postmortems that discuss security vulnerabilities, where that video is stored and who can access it is a critical concern. Most async video tools store everything on their own infrastructure with no option to bring your own storage.

Integration depth matters. Engineers don't context-switch to a separate tool to watch a video. They need recordings embedded directly in Jira tickets, GitHub pull requests, Slack threads, and Linear issues. Surface-level integrations that paste a URL aren't enough — the video should render inline with metadata, timestamps, and chapter markers.

API access matters. Engineering teams automate everything. If your async video tool doesn't have an API that lets you programmatically create, manage, and embed recordings, you can't integrate it into CI/CD pipelines, automated documentation generation, or custom internal tools.

What Matters for Engineering Workflows

Before diving into individual tools, here are the five evaluation criteria we're using, weighted for engineering use cases.

Data sovereignty and self-hosting. Can you control where recordings are stored? Can you run the infrastructure on your own cloud or on-premise? For companies in regulated industries or with strict security policies, this is non-negotiable.

Developer tool integrations. Native integrations with Jira, GitHub, GitLab, Slack, Linear, and Notion — not just link unfurling, but rich embeds with playback controls.

API and extensibility. A documented REST or GraphQL API that lets you programmatically manage the entire recording lifecycle.

Recording quality and features. Resolution, frame rate, annotation tools, code-aware features like syntax highlighting in screenshots, and the ability to record specific windows or browser tabs without capturing your entire desktop.

Pricing model. Per-seat vs. usage-based, free tier limitations, and total cost at engineering team scale (25-200 users).

1. TruCast by TruVideo

TruCast is the async screen recording product from TruVideo, built from the ground up for engineering teams that care about data ownership. Unlike every other tool on this list, TruCast is built on a BYOS (Bring Your Own Storage) architecture — your recordings live in your own S3 bucket, GCS bucket, or Azure blob storage. TruVideo never has access to your content.

Standout features: Self-hosted deployment option for air-gapped environments. API-first design with full programmatic control over recordings. Deep integrations with Jira, GitHub, and Slack that render inline, not as external links. Automatic transcription with searchable, timestamped chapters. Code review mode that syncs video playback with diff views. Incident postmortem templates with structured timeline markers.

Best for: Engineering organizations at companies with strict data policies, SOC 2/ISO 27001 requirements, or government/defense work where recordings containing source code or architecture details cannot leave your infrastructure.

Limitation: TruCast is newer to market than Loom, so the Chrome extension and desktop app are still catching up on polish. The core functionality is rock-solid, but power users who've memorized Loom's keyboard shortcuts may need a brief adjustment period.

Pricing: Free tier for individuals. Team plans start at $8/user/month with unlimited recordings and BYOS included. Enterprise pricing for self-hosted deployments.

2. Zight (formerly CloudApp)

Zight has been in the screenshot-and-screen-recording space for over a decade, rebranding from CloudApp in 2023. It's a mature product with a strong desktop app for macOS and Windows that handles screenshots, GIFs, and video recordings.

Standout feature: Excellent annotation tools for marking up screenshots and video frames. The GIF creation workflow is the best in class if your team uses animated GIFs for bug reports or documentation.

Limitation: No self-hosting option. All content stored on Zight's infrastructure. The API is functional but limited compared to what engineering teams typically need for deep automation. Integrations exist but lack the depth of purpose-built developer tools.

3. Vidyard

Vidyard is primarily a sales video platform that also offers screen recording. It's feature-rich for sales use cases (viewer analytics, CRM integration, video email) but wasn't designed for engineering workflows.

Standout feature: Advanced viewer analytics showing exactly who watched what and for how long. If your use case involves sharing technical demos with external stakeholders, these analytics are valuable.

Limitation: Sales-oriented product design means engineering-specific features are absent. No developer tool integrations beyond basic link sharing. No self-hosting. No BYOS. Pricing is built for sales team budgets, which can be expensive at engineering team scale.

4. ScreenPal

Formerly Screencast-O-Matic, ScreenPal is one of the most affordable screen recording options available. It includes a capable video editor, which is rare in this category, and offers both cloud-hosted and downloadable recording options.

Standout feature: Built-in video editor with trimming, annotation, blur, and overlay capabilities. You can record and produce a polished video without leaving the app. For teams that create documentation videos or tutorials, this is a major time saver.

Limitation: No API. No developer tool integrations. The user interface is dated compared to modern tools. It's a screen recorder with editing, not a team async communication platform. Fine for individuals creating content, less suited for team workflows with shared libraries and collaboration.

5. Tella

Tella takes a different approach to async video — it's designed for creating polished, presentation-style recordings with layouts, backgrounds, and multi-source composition. Think "pitch video creator" more than "quick screen share."

Standout feature: Beautiful multi-layout recording with picture-in-picture, slide integration, and professional-grade output. If you're recording a product demo, investor update, or conference talk, Tella produces the most visually impressive output of any tool here.

Limitation: Overkill for day-to-day engineering communication. When you need to fire off a quick video showing a bug reproduction or a code walkthrough, Tella's layout and composition features add friction. No API, no developer integrations, no self-hosting.

6. Cap (Open Source)

Cap is the open-source entry in the async video space. It's relatively new but has gained traction with developers who want full control over their recording stack. You can self-host the entire platform or use Cap's hosted service.

Standout feature: Fully open source (AGPLv3). You can inspect the code, self-host on your own infrastructure, and modify the platform for your specific needs. For engineering teams that are philosophically committed to open source or have regulatory requirements that mandate code auditability, Cap is the only option that fully satisfies those requirements.

Limitation: Still early-stage. The feature set is more limited than commercial alternatives — basic recording, sharing, and viewing, but limited team management, analytics, and integrations. Self-hosting requires DevOps effort to deploy and maintain. The project is evolving quickly, but you're trading maturity for openness.

7. Vimeo Record

Vimeo Record is Vimeo's screen recording extension, integrated into the broader Vimeo video platform. It's a solid basic recorder with the advantage of Vimeo's proven video hosting infrastructure behind it.

Standout feature: Best-in-class video hosting and playback quality. Vimeo's adaptive streaming, global CDN, and player reliability are unmatched. If video quality and playback performance are your top priorities, Vimeo's infrastructure is the most robust option.

Limitation: The recording tool itself is basic — Chrome extension only, limited annotation, and no engineering-specific features. The value is in Vimeo's hosting platform, not the recording experience. Pricing can escalate quickly on Vimeo's business plans when you factor in storage and team seats.

Head-to-Head Comparison

Here's how the seven alternatives compare across the dimensions that matter most for engineering teams.

Data sovereignty: TruCast (BYOS architecture, self-hosted option) and Cap (self-hostable open source) are the only tools that give you full control over where recordings are stored. Every other tool on this list stores your data on their infrastructure.

API access: TruCast offers a comprehensive API covering the full recording lifecycle. Zight and Vidyard have APIs but with limited scope. ScreenPal, Tella, Cap, and Vimeo Record either have no API or very limited programmatic access.

Developer integrations: TruCast leads with native Jira, GitHub, and Slack integrations designed for inline rendering. Zight and Vimeo offer basic integrations. The others rely on link sharing.

Self-hosting: Only TruCast (commercial, supported) and Cap (open source, community-supported) offer self-hosted deployment.

Price at 50 seats: Cap is free (self-hosted). ScreenPal and TruCast are the most cost-effective commercial options. Vidyard and Vimeo are the most expensive at team scale.

The Verdict

If your engineering team's primary concern is data sovereignty and developer workflow integration, TruCast is the clear choice. It's the only commercial tool built specifically for teams that can't — or won't — hand their source code recordings to a third-party SaaS provider.

If you want full open-source control and have the DevOps capacity to self-host, Cap is worth evaluating — with the caveat that you're adopting an early-stage project.

If you're a small team with simple needs and budget constraints, ScreenPal gets the job done affordably. If you're primarily creating polished external-facing videos, Tella or Vidyard may be better fits.

But for engineering teams that take security seriously, integrate async video into their development workflow, and want an API-first platform they can build on — the Loom era is over. The tools built specifically for how developers work are now better than the general-purpose tool that happened to get popular first.

The best async video tool for your team isn't the one with the most features. It's the one that fits your security requirements, integrates with your existing stack, and disappears into your workflow instead of adding friction to it.
